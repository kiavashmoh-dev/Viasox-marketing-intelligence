# Breakthrough Advertising — Eugene Schwartz (study distillation)
*Part of The Marketing Brain (Portable) — a universal knowledge base. Full book studied; all frameworks preserved.*

**Source type / When to consult:** The foundational strategy text of direct response (1966; Boardroom reissue). THE authority for: choosing an angle/lead; deciding what a headline or hook may say; diagnosing a flopped ad (wrong desire, wrong awareness state, wrong sophistication stage, belief violated, claim unprepared); judging how tired a market is and what claim strength it will still believe; structuring long-form persuasion (VSL, advertorial, sales page) so claims get believed. Everything downstream (Hopkins testing, Bly craft) assumes this layer was gotten right first.

## Core thesis (one tight paragraph)

Advertising does not create desire — it can't afford to. Desire is created by social, economic, and technological forces bigger than any budget; a market is born the moment a private want is shared by a statistically significant number of people. The copywriter's entire job is to *channel* that already-existing mass desire onto one product — which is why $1 of advertising can return $50–100 (the Amplification Effect), but only when it rides existing desire; trying to create desire is education, worth $1 per $1. Execution is analysis, not formula: every product-market-timing relationship is unique, so no formula works twice. Two diagnostics determine everything: how *aware* the prospect is of his desire and your solution (State of Awareness → what the headline says) and how many similar promises he's already heard (State of Sophistication → how the claim is framed). Body copy then fuses the three dimensions of the prospect's mind — Desire, Identification, Belief — into Absolute Conviction, using structure as much as content. (The later preface reframes the book: we are all "Market-Makers," and "people don't change: only the direction of their desires do.")

## The complete frameworks

### 1. Mass desire — channel, never create

- Definition: "the public spread of a private want." A market exists when enough people privately share a desire to profitably repay reaching them.
- You cannot create, diminish, or fight it — Chrysler's short car vs. the long-low-wide trend, Ford selling safety in the horsepower era, and the Edsel all died running against the tide.
- Two force categories: **Permanent forces** — (a) mass instinct (attractiveness, virility, health): the trend is obvious, the job is differentiation, fresh appeal, stronger believability; (b) mass technological problems (bad reception, corroding mufflers): customers buy-and-try until solved — offer the same relief a new way. **Forces of change** — trends (beginning, fulfillment, *reversal*), style, mass education: the job is timing — sense the tide while imperceptible, know which built-in appeal to stress when, be there first.
- **Three dimensions of desire** (choose the desire scoring highest on all three, now): (1) **urgency/intensity** (arthritic pain vs. minor headache); (2) **staying power / inability to satiate** (raw hunger vs. gourmet craving); (3) **scope** — how many share it.
- Every product taps 2–4 desires; **only one can predominate**. That choice is the most important step, embodied in the headline; wrong, and nothing else in the ad matters.
- **Physical vs. functional product:** every product is two products — the thing delivered and the product-in-action. People buy what it *does*; "no physical part of your product can ever become a headline." Physical facts serve later: justify price, document quality, assure continuity, sharpen the mental picture (Rolls-Royce's rubber-shielded chassis), and give the claim a *fresh basis for believability* — the point of difference, often invented by the copywriter and built into the product.
- One product = dozens of performances (a car: transportation, dependability, economy, power, recognition, value, novelty). Feature ONE; the rest documents it. "Every product gives you dozens of keys. But only one will fit the lock."

### 2. The headline's real job

The headline need not sell, name the product, or state the main appeal — that would load the whole sale onto the 10–20% of space it occupies. **Its only job: stop the prospect and compel him to read the second sentence.** Each sentence's job is the next. Sole exception for a full-sell headline: prospects actively seeking your brand, plus a price cut. Its content comes from three answers: the mass desire, the market's Awareness, the market's Sophistication — the last two locate the market *relative to your product* and set the point of entry. The 5–10 headline words are ~90% of the ad's value and may take weeks: "You don't get an idea or a headline—you either build it, or you unfold it, petal by petal."

### 3. The Five States of Awareness (what the headline says)

Each state is walled off from the next; a headline that works in one state fails in another — and a winner expires once its own market moves on.

**1 — Most Aware.** Knows the product, wants it, hasn't bought. Headline = name + bargain: "Revere Zomar Lens… Formerly $149.50—Now Only $119.95." Price is the news; the least creative job in advertising.

**2 — Knows the product, doesn't yet want it.** The bulk of all advertising. Show the name; use the rest of the headline for superiority. Seven tasks: (a) reinforce desire — association ("Steinway—The Instrument of the Immortals"), sensory sharpening ("The skin YOU love to touch"), illustration ("Please don't lick this page"); (b) sharpen the image of how it satisfies ("At 60 miles an hour, the loudest noise in this Rolls Royce is the electric clock"); (c) extend where/when it satisfies ("Thirst knows no season"); (d) new proof/documentation ("9 out of 10 screen stars use Lux"; Jake LaMotta vs. the Mono cup); (e) new mechanism that satisfies better (Hoover washes floors and vacuums the scrub water); (f) new mechanism removing limitations (Zenith's inconspicuous hearing aid); (g) change the image/mechanism entirely to escape competition → the Sophistication problem (framework 4).

**3 — Knows what he wants, not that a product does it.** New-product introduction. Pinpoint the uncrystallized desire, then crystallize it so sharply every prospect recognizes it at a glance. Steps: *name the desire and/or its solution in the headline; prove it can be accomplished; show the mechanism is in your product.* Examples: "Who else wants a whiter wash—with no hard work?"; "How to win friends and influence people"; "To men who want to quit work some day"; "When doctors feel rotten—this is what they do"; "Now! Run your car without spark plugs"; "17,000 blooms from a single plant?" Plain statement when possible; else reinforce with fresh proof, mystery, or wonderment. From here down, execution outweighs mechanics — analysis, intuition, and verbal creativity ("give a name to the still-undefined").

**4 — Has a need, doesn't connect it to the product.** The problem-solving ad: *name the need and/or solution; dramatize the need vividly; present the product as the inevitable solution.* Gradient: bare problem ("Corns?"); problem+solution ("Stops maddening itch"); limitation removal ("Shrinks hemorrhoids without surgery"); substitute for an unpleasant task ("Now! A ring and piston job in a tube!"); prevention via others ("Look, Mom! No cavities!"); emotional sharpening ("Do YOU make these mistakes in English?"); worn-out directness revived by twist ("How a bald-headed barber helped save my hair").

**5 — Completely unaware** — or won't admit the desire, or it's too amorphous or secret to verbalize. Hardest, most valuable, and where "dead" products return (rebirth signals: volume falls as budgets rise; the name, the function statement, then the desire statement all stop selling). Elimination rules: **no price; no product name (wary even of the logo); no direct statement of function, desire, or problem.** What's left is the market itself — the *identification headline*: "You are calling your market together… You are telling them what they are. You are defining them for themselves." It sells only the next paragraph; the body builds a progression: identification → awareness of problem/desire → a solution exists → your product. The named patterns:
- *Hidden dream given words* — "THE UNIVERSITY OF THE NIGHT" (correspondence schools; Lincoln at his log fire).
- *Hidden fear exploited* — "WHY MEN CRACK…" (Postum; revived a brand whose name was a liability).
- *Unacceptable problem entered via accepted image* — "WITHIN THE CURVE OF A WOMAN'S ARM" (Odorono).
- *Unspeakable desire projected visually* — the Marlboro tattoo ads (virility words would be rejected as absurd).
- *Common resentment widening the market* — "WHY HAVEN'T TV OWNERS BEEN TOLD THESE FACTS" (after "Save up to $100 a year on your TV repairs!" failed — it reached only self-identified handymen).
- *Ultimate triumph* — "THEY LAUGHED WHEN I SAT DOWN AT THE PIANO. BUT WHEN I STARTED TO PLAY!—"
- *Result of a problem people reject stated directly* — "OFTEN A BRIDESMAID BUT NEVER A BRIDE" (bad breath "too insulting to be taken straight").
- *Reward projected to hide the work* — "HERE'S AN EXTRA $50, GRACE—" (I.C.S.).
Cardinal rule: "Your prospect must identify with your headline before he can buy from it… It must pick out the product's logical prospects—and reject as many people as it attracts." Never a mere startler, joke, or pretty picture — the easiest headline type to write emptily.
*Style vs. strategy:* styles (verse, confession narrative, comic strip) go stale then laughable; "though styles may change, strategy does not" — the piano and bridesmaid ads rewritten in today's idiom would still sell.

### 4. The Five Stages of Market Sophistication (how the claim is framed)

One research question, answerable in hours: how many similar products came before you? The cigarette industry ran all five stages (taste → comparative enlargement → "toasted"/T-zone mechanisms → mechanism elaboration → identification imagery, then flavor again: "Winston tastes good…"); the filter sub-market retraced the cycle in miniature (Micronite → 20,000 traps → the ¼ inch → dual filter → flavor). "BLOW SOME MY WAY" opened the whole women's market with an identification image when no direct suggestion was printable — "a vast new market—opened up with four words."

**Stage 1 — You're first.** No sophistication; your story is new and believed. *Be simple, direct, never fancy:* name the need or claim, dramatize, bring in the product, prove it. "NOW! LOSE UGLY FAT!" First-ness comes from technology (hair spray, LPs, Model T price) or ad-man insight: new market for an old product (Ovaltine: insomnia aid → body-builder for skinny children), new medium (Revlon on "The $64,000 Question"), newly noticed performance (Lifebuoy's odor → the "B.O." cure).

**Stage 2 — Second in; the claim still works.** *Copy the claim and enlarge it — drive it to the limit; outbid.* "LOSE UP TO 47 POUNDS IN 4 WEEKS—OR RECEIVE $40 BACK!"; roses 25–50–100 → 600 mums → 17,000 blooms; the horsepower race ending in the Chrysler 300, "pegged just at the limit of believability." The stage always disintegrates: words lose meaning, "whiter-than-whites" appear, prospects discount claims 50%, government investigates, sales fall.

**Stage 3 — They've heard all the claims, in all extremes.** The market needs **A NEW MECHANISM** — "a new way to making the old promise work… a brand-new possibility of success where only disappointment has resulted before." Emphasis shifts from what it does to *HOW it works*. Structural signature: the Stage-2 layout is **reversed** — mechanism in the headline, the full claim restated in the lead paragraph. "FLOATS FAT RIGHT OUT OF YOUR BODY!" / "FIRST WONDER DRUG FOR REDUCING!" (then "…melts up to 37 POUNDS off… without starvation diets…"). Note: markets with *restorative power* (recurring instinct, unsolved problems, periodic repurchase) never die — desire never fades; only the approach wears out; the market secretly waits for a new way.

**Stage 4 — Competitors adopt your mechanism.** *Elaborate/enlarge the mechanism:* easier, quicker, surer; solves more; removes old limits; extra benefits. "FIRST NO-DIET REDUCING WONDER DRUG!" Stage-2 logic applied to mechanisms — same fate. Then a genuinely new believable mechanism, or none will be believed.

**Stage 5 — The market no longer believes advertising.** The field "exhausted"; competitors exit en masse. Same strategy as Awareness Stage 5: abandon promise and mechanism; bring the prospect in **through identification with himself** ("WHY MEN CRACK…"; Marlboro's wordless virility).

### 5. The 38 verbalization techniques (strengthening the headline idea)

Verbalization strengthens a claim, freshens it, or pulls the reader into the body — by *how* it's stated. If first in the field, the bare claim is best. The list: (1) measure the claim's size ("20,000 filter traps"); (2) measure its speed ("Feel better fast"); (3) compare it ("Six times whiter"); (4) metaphorize it ("Melts away ugly fat"); (5) sensitize it — feel/smell/touch/see/hear ("Tastes like you just picked it"); (6) demonstrate via prime example (LaMotta; the Rolls clock); (7) dramatize it or its result ("They laughed when…"); (8) state it as paradox ("Beat the races by picking losers"); (9) remove limitations ("…without surgery"); (10) associate with valued people (Mickey Mantle; 9 of 10 decorators); (11) show how much work it does ("all 5 acid-caused stomach troubles—in seconds"); (12) question it ("Who else wants…?"); (13) offer how-to information ("How to…"); (14) tie in authority ("Boss mechanic shows…"); (15) before-and-after (Coldene's two-headline cold); (16) stress newness ("Announcing!"); (17) stress exclusivity ("Only Gleem has GL-70"); (18) turn it into a challenge ("Which twin has the Toni?"); (19) case-history quotation ("Look, Mom—no cavities!"); (20) condense — swap product and what it replaces ("Pour yourself a new engine"); (21) symbolize via parallel reality ("the Atlantic Ocean becomes only one-fifth as long"); (22) connect mechanism to claim ("Floats fat…"); (23) contradict the expected mechanism ("Hit hell out of the ball with your RIGHT hand"); (24) connect need and claim ("Find the man"); (25) offer information in the ad itself ("Why men crack…"); (26) claim/need as case history ("Again she orders—a chicken salad, please"); (27) name the problem ("daytime fatigue"); (28) warn of pitfalls without it ("Don't invest one cent… until…"); (29) emphasize by phraseology — split/repeat ("Nobody but nobody undersells Gimbel's"); (30) ease via a universally-overcome limitation ("If you can count to eleven…"); (31) state the difference outright ("…right in the additives"); (32) surprise — limitations gone ("Crush a Hartman DC-8? Nothing!"); (33) address those who can't buy ("If you've already taken your vacation, don't read this"); (34) address the prospect directly ("To the man who will settle for nothing less than the presidency of his firm"); (35) dramatize production difficulty ("we had to invent a whole new kind of candle"); (36) accuse the claim of being too good ("Is it immoral to make money this easily?"); (37) challenge limiting beliefs ("You are twice as smart as you think"); (38) question-and-answer (United Delco). "Try to create your own—tomorrow."

### 6. Three levels of creativity + supporting doctrine

1. **Word-substitution** into proven headlines → "Echo Ads" that ignore the unique product-market-timing relationship; "the kind of ads that clients write." 2. **Formulas** — valid only for verbalization; "the idea for an ad demands its own shape." 3. **Analysis** — signpost questions; "the art of asking the right questions and letting the problem dictate the right answers." Solutions work once; tired copywriters copy — worst, they copy themselves. "The solution to the unique lies only in itself."
- **Motivation research** feeds hidden desires, taboos, market splits, phrase reactions — "a motivation research finding is not a headline"; it's a direction. The copywriter also runs a years-long discourse with his market, turning failures into research questions.
- **Product personality:** every product has a full personality, but ONE trait always summarizes it (Cadillac = quality, always); feature it, blend others in for variety.
- **Prevention rule:** a man won't visualize future disasters for *himself* — only for wife, children, loved ones (decay sold via children; life insurance via the widow's plight).
- **Splinter markets:** markets aren't monolithic (reducing = appearance vs. health segments — same claim, different mechanisms: speed/ease vs. safety/permanence). A small budget attacks a sociological segment (changing headline, identification, mechanism, media, distribution together); segment success later becomes the point of difference for invading the majority.

### 7. The three dimensions of the prospect's mind

Body copy sells by altering the prospect's vision of reality along three dimensions, which set copy length, structure, style, pace: **Desires** → Intensification; **Identifications** (roles) → Identification; **Beliefs** (his logic, not yours) → Gradualization, Redefinition, Mechanization, Concentration, Camouflage, Verification. Desire + identification must fuse with belief to reach the goal: **Absolute Conviction**.

### 8. Intensification — 13 ways to strengthen desire

Advertising is "salesmanship in print… the literature of desire… society's encyclopedia of dreams." Desires exist as blurs; fill them out with concrete images of fulfillment — "show him in minute detail all the tomorrows that your product makes possible for him." The sharper and more numerous the pictures, the less important the price. You cannot repeat, but you can reinforce. The 13 devices (mail-order examples, where the whole sale happens in one ad):
1. **Direct presentation** — exhaustive description of product/results (the 4,076-roses ad; the Bible projector; the Pan Am model jet).
2. **Claims in action** — exactly how it does it (the jet in flight; the fish lure's buzzing dive-and-climb).
3. **Bring in the reader** — his first day of ownership ("Picture this to yourself! Next weekend…" — the stoplight race, the other driver's startled face).
4. **Turn demonstration into a test** he can visualize running (fire-injector idle/tachometer test; the memory book's twenty-facts-in-one-evening).
5. **Stretch benefits through time** (the English plant pellets' three automatic stages → "blinded by the explosion of color").
6. **Bring in an audience** — celebrities (testimonial) or ordinary people (narration/case history: "They Laughed…"; the reducing case histories).
7. **Experts astonished** — "There is nothing so astounding as the astonishment of experts" (horticulturists watching Nearly Wild out-bloom the prize roses).
8. **Compare/contrast superiority** (Dollar Book Club: $3.95 vs. 99¢, three times running; the Fuel Reclaimer's "free, second gas tank").
9. **Picture the black side** — Heaven-or-Hell: irritate the wound, then the salve; repulsion + attraction (the old plug's "BLACK, FILTHY CARBON" vs. the self-cleaning Power Flash).
10. **Show how easy** — tiny effort vs. huge result ("just 30 seconds work… eight different ways"); every touchpoint is a fresh perspective.
11. **Metaphor, analogy, imagination** (Sherwin Cody's personified "100% Self-Correcting Device" — the mentor forever at your elbow).
12. **The catalog** (summary before closing): *horizontal* — application on application (the blowtorch's "101 jobs"); *vertical* — desire on desire (the fish lure's "frees you forever…" litany). The deliberate last-minute shotgun after the ad's rifle-shot single appeal.
13. **The guarantee as climax** — a final summary of performances, each restated with the money-back reassertion (Power Flash's two-year triple guarantee; the Magic Pellets' "…or every cent of your money back" sequence).
Stop-rule: keep each new perspective only while it renews interest; re-read days later to find where reinforcement became repetition.
**Campaigns:** compress the dominant image into one statement that sells every time, then vary its presentation: ritual repetition + varied opening analogy (Colgate's Gardol shield); "Rule and Example" — fixed line, rotating illustrations (Toni twins; Clairol's "Does she or doesn't she?"); focal-point campaigns with no repeated slogan (Cadillac quality in every element; VW's product-only "Think Small"/"Lemon" building the Honest Car theme). VW headlines also take the **Creative Gamble** — no product or claim in the headline; risk the page to pull the reader into full body copy and double or triple the ad's effectiveness.

### 9. Identification — building a saleable personality into the product

A second species of desire: not for satisfaction but for *expression* — the prospect's wish to act out roles and define himself. Every product should offer fulfillment of a want AND a way of fulfilling it that defines the buyer as a particular kind of person. Two role types:
- **Character roles** (adjectives: progressive, chic, brilliant). A product helps *achieve* the role, *speeds* its mastery, or *symbolizes* it for others. Because character roles are subconscious, unclaimed, and untestable, **prospects believe assigned character roles more readily than performance claims** — but only when implied in images/symbols; verbalized, they turn "corny" and get rejected.
- **Achievement roles** (society's titles: Executive, Wife, $20,000-a-Year Man). Invisible attainments that must be displayed through possessions: "In America today we are known—not only by the company we keep—but by the products we own" (the newlywed's all-new material personality; Ford→Buick→Cadillac). At least half of purchases can't be explained by function alone (the 150-mph sports car on 35-mph parkways = "successful sportsman").
- **The primary image:** every product arrives with an accepted personality (cigarette = virile; piston ring = precision; appliance = time-saving) — "as hard as rock." You cannot contradict it: "people just won't believe that a product is what it isn't." Two moves: (1) change its *intensity* — dramatize if favorable (Marlboro: virile men + virile situations + the tattoo — one emotion symbolized three ways), subdue if negative; (2) use it as a **bridge** into a larger image (Chesterfield's "Blow Some My Way": the male smoker retained but darkened, leading the woman viewer through romance and shared daring into acceptance). Added images must emerge from and stay consistent with the primary image. (Worked example: neutral piston rings glamorized via the Mercedes SL-300, the immaculate sports-car shop, expert mechanic, sportsman-owner — every role implied by visual detail; replacement reframed as installing high-performance equipment.)
- **Limits:** unbelievable identifications kill ads (the deodorant soap selling a society image to a mass market — disbelief spread to the performance claims). Two tests: do people already believe the product has these traits? Can they identify their life, or next step up, with them? If not, insert a believability-bridge first: "If you demand that your prospect jump across a believability-chasm, your ad will fail… build a bridge of ideas… starting on his side… he will let you lead him almost anywhere."
- **Image sources in the physical product:** appearance (borrow from glamour extremes — racing fins onto sedans), components (electronic parts → radar/jets: "offer to let him participate in the frontier explorations of our world"), technical background (Micrin mouthwash bottled in doctors'-office glassware: the package said "This is medicine. This works.").

### 10. Gradualization — the architecture of belief

Belief = the prospect's picture of reality *plus* the emotional security he draws from it; asking him to shatter a belief is asking him to become a child again — he won't. The law, both edges: **violate his beliefs in the slightest degree and nothing you promise can save the ad; channel his existing belief behind one claim and "that one fully-believed claim will sell more goods than all the half-questioned promises your competitors can write"** (properly directed, it supports even otherwise-absurd claims). Belief is immutable to advertising: comply and **extend** — bridge from the facts he accepts today to the facts he must accept to buy.
- **Definition:** open with a statement accepted immediately and entirely; build a chain of acceptances to a goal-conclusion he'd never accept raw. Gradualization governs the ad's **structure, not content** — every statement draws strength from its content AND from the *preparation* made for it, so an ad can be strengthened purely by re-sequencing. "Belief ultimately depends upon structure." Ads are built "not of words—but of reactions."
- **Awareness = readiness to accept.** Hence the split-test-proven paradox: **your most powerful claim does not always make your most powerful headline** — unprepared, it reads as exaggeration. When a power headline fails, run a weaker-but-believed headline and build a belief-bridge to the same claims inside.
- **The TV Manual master dissection:** "Save up to $100 a Year on Your TV Repairs!" failed. The rewrite: suspicion headline ("WHY HAVEN'T TV OWNERS BEEN TOLD THESE FACTS?") crystallizing an existing resentment — no claim, only disclosure → inclusion question ("Was your set purchased after the spring of 1947?" — 95% say yes; a Habit of Agreement begins) → first claim borrowing belief from the "Was your… Then…" contingency grammar, from symptom catalogs triggering "Yes—I have them," and from *withholding the mechanism* (say "repairs" now and the non-handyman turns the page) → yes-train of symptom questions → manufacturers' test-room proof → "If your set were properly cared for…" echoed as the accepted condition proving each next statement → goal-conclusion #1: sets are dependable → pivot paragraph ("And, most important, these experts have discovered that you do not have to be a handyman…") smuggling the new idea in as a restatement via paragraph parallelism, tie-in words ("And," "most important"), and the echoed authority phrase → goal-conclusion #2: he can fix minor troubles himself → syllogistic payoff: "You pay a repairman—not for his work—but for his knowledge. If you had that knowledge yourself—then you would not have to pay him at all."
- **Named devices:** (1) *Inclusion question* ("Ask yourself these nine questions"); (2) *Detailed identification* — catalog his symptoms so precisely he knows you've been there ("I don't care whether your child is six years old or twenty…") — demands real research or the net shatters; (3) *Contradiction of present false beliefs* ("forget everything you have ever heard about what age 'must do'…") — needs strong authority; (4) *The language of logic* — therefore, here's why, the reason is clear, in case after case: words that carry conviction *regardless of attached content*; (5) *Syllogistic thinking* — chains of equation (spark→explosion→power→mileage) creating inevitability: he's been shown proof, not told; (6) *Other belief forms* — contingency structures, repetition-of-proof echoing, promise–belief–promise alternation (no breathing space to question), paragraph parallelism.

### 11. Redefinition — removing objections before they form

Three product drawbacks, three cures — redefinition says the product is *this* rather than *that*, removing the roadblock "before the prospect even knows it exists":
1. **Too complicated → Simplification.** Screaming EASY fails; redefine the field first. The TV ad: set compared to the human body; troubles = "warning signals"/"symptoms"; fixes = "a few minor adjustments on the outside controls"; real breakdowns quarantined to 5%. Sherwin Cody ("a masterpiece of Gradualization… should be memorized"): all of English = 69 words + 12 punctuation principles + 25 typical errors → "Only 15 Minutes a Day." Plus the flip-flop/concept-judo: Lifebuoy's repellent odor redefined as smellable *proof* of odor-killing power (B.O.). "Innovation without acceptance is valueless."
2. **Not important enough → Escalation.** Broaden the benefit horizon; redefine the product's role in his life. English-as-persuasion ("WORD POWER MACHINE"); the 99¢ part that "robs you of the real power and enjoyment" of a $4,000 car; "Your Entire Life Is Spent Trying to Get Others to Do What You Want—Without Friction" (undeniable premise → product becomes life-critical).
3. **Costs too much → switch the comparison standard.** The $1.49 fire injectors: "practically made by hand… would have to sell for as high as $5 each… 30 or 40 dollars…" → "pay for themselves 12 times in one year" → a condition attached ("If you will… tell your friends…") → "You can have" (a favor, not a purchase) → "only $1.49." Ten bargain-signals in two paragraphs, none consciously noticed.
Gradualization works by *arrangement of facts*; Redefinition by *rearrangement of perspective*.

### 12. Mechanization — verbal proof it works

Copy is a silent dialogue; anticipate three reader demands at the exact point they arise: "Tell me more," "Who says so?," and "**How does it work?**" Never whether to include mechanism — only *how much*:
- **Name it** (mechanism already understood from others' ads: "Electronic Light Setter… Push-Button Zomar Lens… Only $135") — bold type, then compete on price. Most catalog/retail copy.
- **Describe it** — promise → reason-why (Hopkins's territory). Rinso 1926: three paragraphs of whiteness promise until the reader's "How?" peaks, then "Dirt floats off—stains go. The secret is simply Rinso…" First rule: mechanism copy **is not scientific discourse** — load it with promise and emotion; the secondary claim sells while proving the primary. (1926's simple mechanism would today need far more — "perhaps even invent a miracle ingredient.")
- **Feature it** — in sophisticated markets the mechanism *becomes* the headline ("Floats fat…"; "…without surgery"): "A NEW WAY… a new chance to satisfy your desire—even if everything else you've tried has failed you." (= Sophistication Stage 3.)
- **Price cuts need mechanisms too** — unjustified discounts aren't believed. Robert Collier's overstocked-Madras-mill letter: weather → desperate mill → quarter-million yards below cost → "A Bargain You May Never Get Again" — the slash arrives six paragraphs in with ten times the believability.

### 13. Concentration — destroying the alternatives

Copy sells *a way of satisfying a desire*, always against competition. Five weapons in order: product superiority ("If your copy is better than your product, send it to your client instead of your prospect, and tell him to make it a reality"); promise superiority; product-role; response/one-upping; and **direct attack**. The first four ignore competitors — right when you dominate or your story is fresh. Attack when your budget is smaller and most prospects are *his* customers: crack the image, shatter loyalty, then re-channel desire.
- Iron rule: **"Never attack a weakness unless you can provide the solution to that weakness at the same time!"** The prospect knows your attack is biased; framed for your benefit it breeds dislike; framed as *his* service — your product removes the weakness — he'll question even ingrained loyalty.
- **Structure #1 — point-for-point alternation:** Bad–Good, Bad–Good (the fire-injector sequence: "A spark plug jumps a spark… A fire injector fires…"; "thin skimpy spark" vs. "heavy powerful flame"; each weakness given its causal mechanism; parallel structure sharpening contrast; capped with authority: "the U.S. Air Force pays premium prices…").
- **Structure #2 — time sequence:** life now with current products vs. after switching (the reducing-pill ad: ordinary plans defined as "completely passive" → "So what happened?" → the diet-collapse story every reader has lived → the buried implication that *the pills failed, not her* → the active mechanism + Nobel authority → back into Intensification).
- Length is irrelevant: "SHRINKS HEMORRHOIDS WITHOUT SURGERY" is complete Concentration in four words. What counts: making him question a habit, shift a loyalty, take a chance.

### 14. Camouflage — borrowing believability from the medium

Readers trust their publication; trust carries from editorial to ads — and the medium's *style itself* acquires "an aura of truth all by itself no matter what material it embraces" (a conditioned believability reflex; medium believability matters more than circulation). Three taps:
1. **Adopt format** — match typography, subheads, layout so there's no mental gear-shift from editorial to ad. The book ad rebuilt feature-for-feature for the Wall Street Journal (Journal type, old-fashioned double subheads, rule bars, ugly line drawing instead of a photo) ≈ doubled believability and ran 19 straight months. Rare Schwartz nod to layout: "A single change in format can add 50% to your readership." Re-do per medium.
2. **Adopt phraseology** — news idiom (byline, dateline: "SKIN SPECIALIST DEMONSTRATES… New York, N.Y.—A leading doctor today showed…" → a report, not a sales story); in direct mail, mimic mail that's always opened (checks, government letters, dividend notices, newsletters); on TV/radio, the news sound and documentary feel. "Make your ads blend in… Camouflage them."
3. **Adopt counter-advertising moods** — "advertising language" triggers automatic skepticism by its appearance. (a) *Understatement*: few adjectives, short falling sentences, self-kidding (VW: "There is no radiator. Or hoses. Or water pump. Or rust."); rarity is its power. (b) *Deadly Sincerity*: flag the flaws so the benefits are believed doubly ("This is a private advertisement. It is not meant for ninety-nine men out of every hundred…" — the presidency ad).

### 15. The final touches — assembling the ad

- **Verification** — the *placement* of proof (stats, tests, testimonials, authorities) so it lands "when the reader unconsciously demands it." Proof is selling copy — short, dramatic, specific, never a dead "Here's Proof" box. Documentation is inherently dull: stage it so it "enters as the hero" of a drama your copy built (the test rooms arrive exactly when the TV reader begs for a solution; the Air Force line lands atop accumulated believability). Position increases power.
- **Reinforcement** — "In mathematics, one plus one always equals two—never more. In emotional writing, one plus one can often equal ten." Joined images multiply (Avis: "We're only second" + "We try harder"); flaw-admission jammed against a claim detonates it ("This is John Horn's first book… it has flaws. But there are whole chapters… that may open doors to you—tomorrow"). Creativity = "the ability to combine separate images into a new identity… more powerful than the mere logical sum of its parts."
- **Interweaving** — every sentence carries promise + image + logic at once (the spark-plug sentence packing six disadvantage-images; its mirror packing five promises; the annotated pivot sentence carrying mood, authority, idiom, core promise, restatement, ease, condensation simultaneously). Learned like a golf swing: deliberate "New Awkwardness" practiced into intuition (Creativity #1 → #2). Source: Walter S. Campbell, *Writing Non-Fiction*.
- **Sensitivity — the ad's architecture** — anticipate shift points where the reader says "I've read enough of this; give me that," and switch copy type (promise → mechanism → documentation → promise). Four dissected book-ad structures run from pure promise-momentum (zero documentation) to skepticism-anticipating structures that agree with the doubt, then hand twelve paragraphs to the authority. Dead copy = "You have gone on straight when he wants to turn left." And: **"A new structure is a new ad"** — if the story won't develop, "perhaps the very elements that are begging to come out of your 6-point type should be at the top of your ad."
- **Momentum** — copy's first job is to be read. (1) Momentum phrases at transitions ("Let me explain." "Here's how:" "And yet it's only the beginning."); (2) incomplete statements/teasers — create interest, raise a question, imply the answer comes later ("…nothing more than ordinary tap water, your own ten fingers, and the contents of your garden and your refrigerator"; the attachment "BANNED from the great European raceways") — each simultaneously loaded with sell. Every element does two jobs.
- **Mood** — "Words and rhythms… are to the copy writer what line and color are to the painter." *Image-sharpeners* (visual) vs. *emotion-definers* (evaluative — "precious… blinded… you never imagined before") that tell the reader what to feel while staying invisible. Power words build potency; man-to-man phrases build frankness; machine-gun verb-claim rhythm propels; VW's adjective absence builds trust; the fire injector's clumsy unscannable sentences read as honest, un-copywritten appraisal. Mood-building is "deliberate on the part of the copywriter—unnoticed on the part of his reader"; **if the reader notices your emotion words the copy turns corny and you've lost him** — tune tone per audience and per medium, down as well as up.
- **The library (epilogue):** Watkins's *100 Greatest Advertisements*; Hopkins's *My Life in Advertising*; Kennedy's *Intensive Advertising*; *The Lasker Story*; Collier's *Letter Book*; Ogilvy's *Confessions*; Reeves's *Reality in Advertising*; Campbell; Caples, Young, Glim, Bedell; Dichter, Martineau; Baker's *Visual Persuasion* — and above all the ads themselves.

## Rules & laws (numbered)

1. Copy cannot create desire; it can only channel existing mass desire. Never run against the tide.
2. Creating desire = education = ~$1 per $1; channeled desire = the Amplification Effect.
3. One dominant desire per ad (scored on urgency, staying power, scope); the choice lives in the headline; wrong = nothing else matters.
4. The physical product never headlines; it justifies, documents, assures, sharpens, and lends believability.
5. The headline's only job: stop the prospect and force the second sentence; every sentence's job is the next.
6. A headline built for one awareness state fails in another — and expires when its market moves on.
7. Unaware (Stage-5) markets: no price, no product name, no direct statement of function, desire, or problem in the headline.
8. The prospect must identify with the headline before he can buy; it must reject as many people as it attracts.
9. No formula works twice; only analysis of the unique product-market-timing relationship transfers. Never copy others — or yourself.
10. First in the market: be simple and direct — bare claim, dramatize, prove.
11. Second, claim still working: copy it and enlarge it to the believable limit.
12. Claims exhausted (Stage 3): a new mechanism — into the headline, claim restated beneath.
13. Mechanisms copied: elaborate the mechanism (Stage 4); nothing believed (Stage 5): sell identification, not desire.
14. Your most powerful claim does not always make your most powerful headline; unprepared claims read as lies.
15. Violate the prospect's beliefs in the slightest degree and the ad dies; extend beliefs, never fight them.
16. One fully-believed promise outsells ten partially-believed promises; strengthen preparation before adding claims.
17. Prove with the prospect's kind of logic, not your own.
18. Never contradict a product's accepted primary image — subordinate and bridge from it; never ask the prospect to jump a believability-chasm.
19. Character roles are implied in images/symbols; verbalized, they turn corny and are rejected.
20. Prevention sells only via disasters to loved ones, never to the prospect himself.
21. Mechanism copy must sell — never dull science; secondary claims prove while selling.
22. Price cuts and low prices must be justified by a mechanism or they aren't believed.
23. Never attack a competitor's weakness without presenting your solution simultaneously, framed for the prospect's benefit.
24. Match the medium's format, phraseology, and tone; "ad language" triggers automatic skepticism.
25. Place proof where the reader demands it; documentation must be staged as the hero, never boxed.
26. Keep each fresh perspective only while it renews interest; when reinforcement becomes repetition, cut (re-read days later).
27. Every sentence carries promise, image, and logic together; every element does two jobs.
28. If readers notice your emotion words, the copy is corny — rewrite fast; tune mood per audience and medium.
29. If your copy is better than your product, send the copy to the client and tell him to build it into the product.
30. A new structure is a new ad; if copy dies mid-write, re-architect — the buried 6-point element may be the headline.

## Exact language worth keeping (verbatim, with why)

1. "Copy cannot create desire for a product. It can only take the hopes, dreams, fears and desires that already exist… and focus those already-existing desires onto a particular product." — kills every "create demand" brief.
2. Mass desire "is the public spread of a private want." — the cleanest definition of a market.
3. "Your headline has only one job—to stop your prospect and compel him to read the second sentence of your ad." — the anti-cleverness law for hooks.
4. "If your first thought holds him, he will read the second. If the second holds him, he will read the third." — the model for the first three seconds of any video ad.
5. "Every product gives you dozens of keys. But only one will fit the lock." — one dominant appeal, always.
6. "A headline which will work to a market in one stage of awareness will not work to a market in another stage of awareness." — run this diagnosis before blaming creative quality.
7. "You never step in the same river twice. No market ever stands still." — fatigue is structural; iteration isn't optional.
8. "Your prospect must identify with your headline before he can buy from it… It must… reject as many people as it attracts." — targeting is done by the copy, not the platform.
9. "What this market needs now is… A NEW MECHANISM—a new way to making the old promise work." — the operational sentence for tired markets.
10. "These five or ten words will make up about 90% of the value of your ad." — where the hours belong.
11. "Advertising is the literature of desire… society's encyclopedia of dreams." — the job description for body copy; you are "the script writer for your prospect's dreams."
12. "You cannot repeat, but you can reinforce." — the line between intensification and boredom.
13. "There is nothing so astounding as the astonishment of experts." — a proof device in one sentence.
14. "In America today we are known—not only by the company we keep—but by the products we own." — role-selling in one line.
15. "If you violate your prospect's established beliefs in the slightest degree… nothing you promise him, no matter how appealing, can save your ad." — audit every claim against this.
16. "One fully-believed promise has ten times the sales power of ten partially-believed promises." — the case for cutting claims, not adding.
17. "You pay a repairman—not for his work—but for his knowledge." — model syllogistic payoff closing a gradualized argument.
18. "Never attack a weakness unless you can provide the solution to that weakness at the same time!" — the attack rule.
19. "In emotional writing, one plus one can often equal ten." — juxtaposition as the core creative act.
20. "A new structure is a new ad." — the cheapest iteration lever: re-architect before re-conceiving.

## How to apply this to any brand

**The diagnostic procedure (run before any creative work):**

1. **Name the mass desire you're channeling.** Write it as "the public spread of a private want" — whose private want, spread across how many people? Score the candidate desires on urgency, staying power, and scope; pick ONE to predominate. If the brief says "create demand," stop — find the existing desire instead.
2. **Diagnose the awareness state of each audience segment** (most-aware → completely unaware) and write the hook that state permits: price/name for most-aware; superiority/proof for product-aware; crystallize-the-desire for desire-aware; name-and-dramatize-the-problem for need-aware; identification-only (no price, no name, no direct promise) for unaware.
3. **Place the market on the sophistication curve** — count how many similar promises your buyer has already heard. Stage 1–2: direct claims, then enlargement to the believable limit. Stage 3: new mechanism in the headline, claim beneath. Stage 4: elaborate the mechanism. Stage 5: abandon claims for identification. Watch for the **sub-market reset**: a genuinely new sub-niche inside a saturated category re-runs the cycle from Stage 1.
4. **Architect belief before claiming** (gradualization): open on what the prospect already accepts; yes-train; withhold the mechanism until the reader demands it; place proof where it's begged for; close with a syllogism.
5. **Pre-empt the three objections with the three cures**: too complicated → simplify the field; not important → escalate its role in his life; too expensive → switch the comparison standard and give the price a mechanism.
6. **Sell the role in images, never words** — decide what the purchase says about the buyer, then imply it visually/symbolically; audit that nothing contradicts the product's primary image.
7. **Camouflage per medium** — make the ad match the format, phraseology, and mood of the content the audience already trusts on that platform.

**Worked micro-examples:**
- **DTC supplement in a saturated niche (Stage 4–5):** the market has heard every claim and most mechanisms. Either elaborate a mechanism competitors can't copy (sourcing, dosage-form) or go identification: define the buyer to herself and let the body copy walk to the product ("WHY MEN CRACK" pattern).
- **B2B SaaS entering a new category (Stage 1, need-aware):** be simple and direct — name the problem, dramatize the cost of it, present the tool as inevitable. No cleverness; the bare claim is fresh here.
- **High-ticket coaching/service ($5k–$50k):** the price objection is structural → comparison-standard switch (cost of the status quo per month, value of one client/outcome) + a mechanism for the price (capped intake, hands-on delivery); the VSL is a gradualization exercise with two goal-conclusions accepted before the offer appears.
