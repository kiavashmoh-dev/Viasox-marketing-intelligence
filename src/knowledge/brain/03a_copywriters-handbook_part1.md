# The Copywriter's Handbook — Robert Bly (study distillation, Part 1: first half of book)
*Part of The Marketing Brain (Portable) — a universal knowledge base. First half of the 4th edition studied; all frameworks, checklists, hard rules, and examples preserved.*

**Covers:** Prefaces; Ch. 1 An Introduction to Copywriting; Ch. 2 Writing to Get Attention (Headlines & Subject Lines); Ch. 3 Writing to Communicate; Ch. 4 Writing to Sell; Ch. 5 Getting Ready to Write; Ch. 6 Writing Print Advertisements; Ch. 7 Writing Direct Mail; Ch. 8 Writing Brochures, Catalogs & Sales Materials; Ch. 9 Writing Public Relations Materials; opening of Ch. 10 Writing TV & Radio Commercials (the 12 commercial types begin here — demonstrations and testimonials covered; rest in Part 2).

**When to consult:** Before writing or reviewing ANY ad copy, hook, headline, subject line, VSL opening, landing page lead, or script line. This is the craft layer: how to get attention, how to be clear, how to persuade, how to prepare. If a hook feels weak, a script feels bloated, or copy "sounds like advertising," the diagnosis is almost certainly in here.

## Complete craft frameworks

### 1. The copywriter's identity and the 4 jobs of copy
"A copywriter is a salesperson behind a typewriter" (Judith Charles). The biggest mistake is judging advertising as laypeople do — by whether it's liked, clever, or entertaining. The goal is sales. For copy to convince a consumer to buy, it must do four things, in order: **(1) Get attention. (2) Communicate. (3) Persuade. (4) Ask for a response.** Digital metrics make this unforgiving: when page views, time on page, CTR, conversions and sales are measurable, "creativity" can't be defended if the numbers underperform.

### 2. The three keys to copy in the digital age
Copy today is driven by three factors, not one:
1. **Human emotion** — unchanged for centuries. Hopkins: "Human nature is perpetual… the principles of psychology are fixed and enduring."
2. **Data** — "quantitative measurement based on live data and tests trumps subjective opinion every time." Platform-specific data beats copywriter instinct (e.g., a client's testing showed 8–12 words was the optimal headline length in their niche — data trumps training).
3. **Compliance** — platform ad rules (Meta, Google) gate whether your ad runs at all. Bly's rule of thumb: **for each 10 percent closer you get to perfect compliance, response drops ~10 percent.** Overly zealous compliance produces weak copy; smart marketers find honest, clever ways to get bold, specific ads approved (e.g., swapping direct claims for curiosity-based angles).

### 3. The "first impression" — where attention is won per medium
The first thing seen decides success: print ad = headline + visual; website = home page headline; direct mail = envelope/first sentences; sales presentation = first few slides; **online video = the first twenty seconds**; e-mail = From line + Subject line. A sales letter has ~5 seconds to earn attention. Ogilvy: 5x as many people read the headline as body copy — "When you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents out of your dollar." Changing only the headline has multiplied an ad's selling power tenfold.

### 4. The four functions of a headline
1. **Get attention** — via self-interest/benefit, news (new, discover, introducing, announcing, now, at last), or "free" and other power words (how to, why, you, sale, quick, easy, guarantee, results, proven, save). Never use attention gimmicks unrelated to the product: they attract curious bystanders, not customers.
2. **Select the audience** — call out who the ad is for and screen out non-prospects ("To Men and Women over 65 Who Need Affordable Life Insurance Coverage").
3. **Deliver a complete message** — 4 out of 5 read only the headline, so put the selling promise (and often brand name) in it.
4. **Draw the reader into the body copy** — arouse curiosity with news, intrigue, a question, a provocative statement, or a promised reward ("The $5 Alternative to Costly Plastic Surgery" — note it withholds *what* the alternative is).

### 5. The eight basic headline types
1. **Direct** — states the proposition plainly ("Pure Silk Blouses — 30 Percent Off").
2. **Indirect** — curiosity first, answer in the body copy (double meanings that force reading).
3. **News** — announce something new/improved.
4. **How-to** — "magic" words; the promise of solid information and solutions. When stuck, start typing "How to…"
5. **Question** — must be a question the reader empathizes with or wants answered; focused on the READER's self-interest, never the advertiser's ("Do You Know What the XYZ Company Is Up to?" = "Who cares?").
6. **Command** — start with a strong verb demanding action ("Try Burning This Coupon").
7. **Reason-why** — "5 Reasons Why…", "7 steps", "6 ways" — pairs with listicle body copy; numbers attract the eye and should be written as numerals.
8. **Testimonial** — customers do the selling; use their own unpolished words in quotation marks. **The "unattributed testimonial" trick:** quotation marks around any strong headline catch the eye and make it read as factual even without attribution.

### 6. The 38 swipe-file headline approaches (condensed)
Ask a question; tie into current events; create new terminology; give news; command; use numbers/statistics; promise useful info; highlight the offer; tell a story; make a recommendation; state a benefit; make a comparison; use visualization words; use a testimonial; offer a free report/catalog; state the proposition plainly; arouse curiosity; promise a secret; be specific ("At 60 Miles an Hour, the Loudest Noise in This New Rolls-Royce Comes from the Electric Clock"); target a reader type; add a time element; stress savings/value; give good news; offer an alternative; issue a challenge; stress the guarantee; state the price; set up a seeming contradiction ("Profit from 'Insider Trading' — 100% Legal!"); offer an exclusive; address the reader's concern; "crazy as it sounds…"; make a big promise; show ROI; reasons-why; answer key questions; stress premium value; help achieve a goal; make a contradictory promise ("Cool Any Room Fast — Without Air Conditioning!"). Keep a swipe file; mark ads you see repeatedly (repetition = profitability).

### 7. The 4 U's formula (Mark Ford)
Strong headlines/subject lines are **Urgent, Unique, Ultra-specific, Useful**. Rate every headline 1–4 on each U; if it doesn't score 3–4 on at least three U's, rewrite. Worked example: "Free Special Report" scores 1-2-2-2 → fix by specifying topic and benefit ("Free special report reveals how to cut training costs up to 90% with e-learning"). Apply to headlines, e-mail subject lines, envelope teasers, leads, subheads, and bullets. Never defend a weak headline by pointing to good response — imagine the response with a strong one.

### 8. Bly's headline production technique
Ask four questions: (1) Who is my customer? (2) What are the important product features? (3) **Which of these features do competing products lack?** (4) Why will the customer buy (which feature delivers the most important benefit)? Answer #3 = your key selling proposition; then state it clearly and compellingly. Don't force-fit a formula — start with the sales message. Write 6–8 headlines minimum; use discards as subheads. When stuck: make a word list related to the product and mix-and-match ("Introducing a New Twist in Splint Technology"). Never let headline block you — write body copy and headlines will emerge.

### 9. The 11 rules of writing to communicate (Ch. 3)
1. **Put the reader first** — the "you-orientation." Rewrite advertiser-centric copy into reader benefit ("The objective of the fund is maximum current income consistent with low capital risk" → "The cash fund gives you the maximum return with the lowest risk").
2. **Carefully organize selling points** — headline states the main proposition, first paragraphs expand it, secondary points follow. (The bank that buried "FREE $10" in 4,500 words of a booklet got zero requests.)
3. **Break writing into short sections** — numbered if sequential, bullets if not; subheads for long sections.
4. **Use short sentences** — best average for business writing: **14–16 words**; above 40 = unreadable. Vary length for rhythm. Break long sentences in two; use dashes and ellipses.
5. **Use small, simple words** — "utilize" → "use"; "optimum" → "best". Caples: even the best-educated don't resent simple words, and they're the only ones many people understand.
6. **Avoid jargon** — two rules: don't use a technical term unless 95%+ of readers understand it, and don't use it unless it precisely communicates your meaning.
7. **Be concise** — rewriting is the key; kill redundancies ("free gift," "advance planning," "whether or not").
8. **Be specific** — "the surest way to arouse and hold attention is by being specific, definite, and concrete" (Strunk & White). Collect twice as much material as you'll use.
9. **Go straight to the point** — start selling with the first line. Delete warm-up paragraphs from the final draft.
10. **Write in a friendly, conversational style** — "write like you talk" (Ann Landers). Use pronouns, colloquialisms, contractions. Test: would you ever SAY this to the reader? If natural conflicts with grammatical, write naturally.
11. **Avoid sexist language** — plurals, rewrites, alternating gender, imaginary named persons.

**The 4 S Formula:** Short words. Short sentences. Short paragraphs. Short sections.

**Tricks of the trade:** end sentences with prepositions (conversational); sentence fragments for drama and pace; begin sentences with And/Or/But; occasional one-sentence paragraph as a "sudden curve in the road"; sparing graphic emphasis (underline, bold, P.S.); **bullet-writing rule (Parris Lampropoulos): be specific about the problem, vague and mysterious about the solution, with a twist** ("How a pickpocket can make your back pain better").

### 10. Features → Benefits
A feature is what the product is/has; a benefit is what the user gains. "Customers don't buy products or services. They buy what these products and services are going to do for them." Two-column exercise: list every feature on the left, and for each ask "What does this do for the customer?" on the right (the No. 2 pencil demo: hexagonal cylinder → won't roll off your desk). The benefits column = your sales points; the strongest one = your headline theme.

### 11. The Motivating Sequence (and its cousins)
Formulas for structuring any persuasive piece: **AIDA** (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action), **ACCA** (Awareness, Comprehension, Conviction, Action), **4 P's** (Picture, Promise, Prove, Push), and Bly's favorite, the five-step **Motivating Sequence**:
1. **Get attention** — headline/visual carries the single strongest benefit. Never save the best for a windup finish.
2. **Show a need** — the need is often not obvious; dramatize the problem the product solves.
3. **Satisfy the need** — position your product as the solution.
4. **Prove it** — benefits, testimonials, comparisons, studies (for health: randomized controlled trials in peer-reviewed journals), company stability.
5. **Ask for action** — tell the reader the exact next step, make it easy, and give an incentive to act NOW.

### 12. False logic (Mark Ford)
Copy that skillfully arranges true facts so readers draw favorable conclusions ("Not one person in 1,000 has ever tasted them" = exclusivity, not unpopularity; a $99 newsletter framed against $2,000 money-management fees). People buy on emotion and rationalize with logic — so provide credible-seeming justification for what the reader already wants to do. Copywriters are advocates, like lawyers; use every honest tool.

### 13. The USP (Rosser Reeves) — three requirements
1. Each ad must make a **proposition**: "Buy this, get this specific benefit."
2. It must be one the competition **cannot or does not offer**.
3. It must be **strong enough to move the masses**.
If no proprietary advantage exists, claim what competitors have but haven't said ("a claim not otherwise made in that particular form of advertising" — the beer whose bottles were "washed in live steam," true of all beer, claimed by one). MacDougall's four ways to advertise seemingly similar products: (1) stress an underpublicized benefit, (2) dramatize/demonstrate a big benefit compellingly (Flex Tape's sawed boat), (3) dramatize the name or package, (4) build long-term brand personality.

### 14. Big promise + Secondary promise
"Promise, large promise, is the soul of an advertisement" (Samuel Johnson). Small promises don't work in consumer direct marketing — but big promises trigger skepticism. Solution: pair the big promise with a **secondary promise** — a lesser benefit big enough to justify the purchase by itself, small enough to be instantly believed. Skeptics think: "Even if the big claim isn't true, it's worth it for the secondary promise alone — so either way, I can't lose."

### 15. Know your customer: three levels + BDF
Copy should reach prospects on three levels: **intellectual** (logic-based, weakest), **emotional** (fear, greed, love, vanity, benevolence), and **personal** (their own lived experience: "Did you lose a small fortune in the 2008 crash?"). To get there, write out the market's **BDF — Beliefs, Desires, Feelings** — before writing copy, and get the team to agree on it. Classic proof: IT-trainer letter built on IT people's feelings of being disliked and unappreciated — "Important news for any IT professional who has ever felt like telling an end user, 'Go to hell'" — the company's most successful letter ever. Also: sell different benefits to different buyer segments (marine vs. chemical-plant buyers of the same water purifier care about totally different things — ASK). Hauptman: "Start with the prospect, not the product."

### 16. The 22 copy motivators
People buy: to be liked, appreciated, right, important; to make money, save money, save time, make work easier; to be secure, attractive, sexy, comfortable, distinctive, happy; to have fun, gain knowledge, be healthy, gratify curiosity; for convenience; out of fear, greed, guilt.

### 17. Long vs. short copy — the Copy Length Grid
Copy length = f(product, audience, purpose). The grid's two axes: **emotion** (how emotional is the purchase) and **involvement** (how much time/thought goes into it). High emotion + high involvement (engagement ring, career program) → LONG copy. Low/low (paper clips) → short. Modifiers: **Price** (the more expensive, the more copy needed to build value before revealing price); **Purpose** (one-step/mail-order copy must be long and overcome every objection; two-step lead-gen copy can be short); **Audience** (busy executives → shorter; hobbyists/high-interest → longer; book-club buyers read 12-page letters); **Importance** (needed products sell with short copy; wanted-but-optional products must be *sold* with long copy); **Familiarity** (known products need less). "Don't be afraid of long copy. Include as many facts as it takes to make the sale."

### 18. Getting ready to write (Ch. 5)
- **Research:** Get ALL previously published material from the client (ads, brochures, testimonials, competitor files, reviews, specs); it supplies ~90% of what you need; interviews fill the last 10%.
- **The four question sets:** product questions (features/benefits, differentiation, proof, guarantee), audience questions (who buys, why now, main concern, buying influences), objective of the copy (leads, sales, conversions, etc.).
- **Interviews:** phone is fine; e-mail questions in advance; keep notes and sources 6–12 months; document every factual claim (footnotes).
- **Copy platform:** show the client a working headline + outline of theme/contents and get approval BEFORE the first draft.
- **Three-stage writing process:** (1) get it all down, no self-editing; (2) edit for flow, cut needless words, read aloud; (3) clean up — proofread, verify facts, enforce consistency. "The key to writing great copy is rewriting."
- **9-step idea generation:** identify the problem; assemble pertinent facts; gather general knowledge; look for combinations; sleep on it; use a checklist; get feedback; team up; give new ideas a chance (separate idea-producing from editing/criticizing).

### 19. The 9 criteria of a successful print ad (Ch. 6)
1. Headline contains benefit, news, curiosity, or promised reward.
2. Visual (if any) illustrates the headline's main benefit (before/after photos are the strongest form). Words, not pictures, do most of the selling.
3. Lead paragraph expands the headline's theme.
4. Layout draws the reader in: one central visual, bold headline, readable type, black on white, short lead paragraph (<3 lines), no reverse type/slanted copy/tiny type.
5. Body copy covers all sales points in logical sequence (inverted pyramid; list format; problem/solution; chronological for case histories).
6. Copy gives enough information for the next step in the buying process.
7. Copy is interesting ("You cannot bore people into buying your product"). Interesting = speaks to the reader's life, tells a story, is about people, sounds like a letter from a friend. Boring = talks about the company, explains how it's made not what it does, features without benefits, no point of view.
8. Copy is believable — testimonials, demonstrations, evidence, and above all telling the truth. "Clever advertising can convince people to try a bad product once. But it can't convince them to buy a product they've already tried and didn't like."
9. The ad asks for action and names the next step.
Also: full-page ad response declines with each insertion (cream-skimming); classified/fractional ad response can hold steady for years. Two-step metrics: cost per inquiry and inquiry-to-order conversion.

### 20. Direct mail essentials (Ch. 7) — the richest analog for e-mail + retargeting
- Mail is a **personal medium**: signed, first-person, "I" to "you," conversational, brimming with personality. Write it like a letter to a friend.
- **"The letter sells; the brochure tells."**
- **15 ways to start a sales letter:** state the offer; highlight the free literature; make an announcement; tell a story; flatter the reader; **write peer-to-peer** (a farmer letter signed by a farmer: "I am like you. I know your problems. I've been through them myself"); personal message from the president; provocative quote; ask a question; make it personal; **identify the reader's problem** (then solve it); stress a benefit; use human interest ("When I was 16, my father died of a heart attack…"); let the reader in on inside information; sweepstakes.
- **Envelope teasers:** use one only if irresistible; a weak teaser reduces response below a plain envelope. Three formats: boast (worst), benefit promise (better), free-reward-inside (best). Blind envelopes mimicking personal mail often win.
- **Response boosters:** always include a response mechanism; premiums; time limits; guarantee (15–90 days); "no salesperson will call"; a P.S. (**80 percent of readers read the P.S.**); repeat the offer on the reply card; plain-English terms; make responding easy.
- Marketing tactics are cyclical: overuse → abandonment → scarcity → effectiveness again.

### 21. Brochure / long-form sales asset rules (Ch. 8)
Know where the piece fits in the buying process (leave-behind, point-of-sale, inquiry fulfillment, DM insert, sales support); know what other assets support it; know your audience's informational appetite; put a strong selling message on the cover (a cover with only a logo wastes the ad's headline); give complete information (white space worship is folly); organize as a story with beginning/middle/end; short sections with story-telling subheads; hardworking visuals with captions (**captions get twice the readership of body copy**); tell the reader the next step; don't omit the obvious (contact info, guarantee, directions); make it worth keeping (embed useful reference content). White papers = soft-sell brochures in editorial disguise; must contain genuine how-to value.

### 22. PR and earned-media writing (Ch. 9)
Press releases must contain NEWS or useful service information, never warmed-over ads; headline sums the story in 2–3 lines; lead carries who/what/when/where/why/how; one to two pages; hang claims on a quoted person. Publicity is more credible than advertising because readers don't know it's sourced from releases. No news? Manufacture a hook (Domino's "Pizza Meter"). Four feature-article types: case histories (success stories — credible because specific and narrative), how-to (content marketing; clipped and saved), issue pieces, news. Query letters must demonstrate the writing they promise (Bly's AIDA query: "Is this letter a waste of paper?"). Speeches: **120 words per minute — a 20-minute talk is 2,400 words**; one theme; conversational tone; self-effacing humor in small doses; max 5 bullets × 5 words per slide.

### 23. Commercials (Ch. 10 opening)
Two schools: "creative" (stands out, doesn't sell) vs. straightforward demonstration and honest selling (OxiClean, My Pillow). There is no correlation between someone liking a commercial and being sold by it. Today's synthesis: **"edutainment"** — education plus entertainment; serious prospects are information seekers. Format 1: **demonstrations** (show it working; side-by-side comparisons; dramatize the proof). Format 2: **testimonials** (real users beat paid actors; hidden-camera authenticity; unpolished words).

## Checklists

### The Copywriter's Checklist (run before releasing any copy)
- Does the copy fulfill the promise of the headline?
- Is it interesting? ("You can't bore people into buying.")
- Is it easy to read? (Short sentences, short paragraphs, small words.)
- Is it believable? (Testimonials, demonstration, evidence — and truth.)
- Is it persuasive? (Attention → interest → desire → proof → action.)
- Is it specific? (Facts, features, benefits, savings.)
- Is it concise? (When you're finished, stop.)
- Is it relevant? (Don't talk about yourself; talk about what the reader needs.)
- Does it flow smoothly? (No awkward phrases or strange terms.)
- Does it call for action? (Name the next step and tell the reader to take it.)

### Headline evaluation checklist
- Promises a benefit or reward for reading?
- Clear, direct, instantly graspable?
- As specific as possible? ("Lose 19 Pounds in Three Weeks" beats "Lose Weight Fast")
- Grabs attention with a strong sales message, freshly stated?
- Relates logically to the product (no bait-and-switch sensationalism)?
- Headline and visual form a single selling concept?
- Arouses curiosity / pulls the reader into body copy?
- Selects the audience?
- Brand/advertiser name included (when prospects won't read on)?
- No blind headlines, no irrelevant wordplay or puns, no negatives ("100% Sodium-Free," not "Contains No Sodium").

### The 4 U's scoring drill
Rate the headline 1–4 on Urgent / Unique / Ultra-specific / Useful → rewrite until at least three score 3+.

### Pre-writing assignment checklist (condensed from Ch. 5)
- Collected all prior ads, brochures, testimonials, reviews, competitor materials?
- Product Qs answered: features/benefits list, most important benefit, exclusive features, positioning, proof, price, guarantee?
- Audience Qs answered: who buys, why, why now, main concern, character, motivators, all buying influences?
- Objective defined (leads, sales, conversions, image)?
- Copy platform (headline + outline) approved by client before drafting?
- Every fact source documented?

### Direct-mail response booster checklist (abridged)
Response mechanism in every piece; premium offered; something free for responding; time limit on the offer; money-back guarantee; "no obligation / no salesperson will call"; P.S. restating offer; offer repeated on reply form; action words on the reply device; plain-English terms; easy-to-complete response path.

### Brochure completeness proofing list
Logo/name/address; phone; e-mail; directions; prices/hours/locations; ordering instructions; credit cards accepted; guarantees/warranties; shipping/service info; legal marks; URL; spelling/grammar pass.

## Hard rules (numbered)

1. You are a salesperson, not an artist. Grade your copy on sales generated, not originality.
2. The headline is 80 percent of the money spent — never publish with a weak one, and never defend one by pointing to a merely acceptable response.
3. If forced to choose between clever-but-obscure and simple-but-straightforward, be simple and straightforward.
4. Headline must score 3–4 on at least three of the 4 U's.
5. Don't write blind headlines; don't use puns, gimmicks, or attention devices unrelated to the product.
6. Write benefits, not features — translate every feature via the two-column exercise.
7. Lead with your single strongest benefit. Never bury it for a "big finish."
8. Start selling with the very first line. Delete all warm-up from the final draft.
9. Average sentence length 14–16 words; no jargon unless 95%+ of readers understand it AND it's the precise term.
10. Be specific everywhere — specifics sell; vagueness kills belief.
11. Use "you." Write like you talk. Read it aloud; if you wouldn't say it, rewrite it.
12. Every piece of copy ends with a call for action plus an easy response mechanism and a reason to act now.
13. Big promise always accompanied by a believable secondary promise when skepticism is likely.
14. Write the BDF (beliefs, desires, feelings) of the target market BEFORE writing copy.
15. Long copy for high-emotion, high-involvement, high-price, unfamiliar, one-step sales; short copy for lead-gen where a later step completes the sell.
16. Get the copy platform (headline + outline) approved before drafting long-form.
17. In bullets/fascinations: specific about the problem, mysterious about the solution.
18. Tell the truth. Proof beats claims; testimonials in the customer's own unpolished words.
19. Compliance is mandatory but calibrated — every 10% toward perfect compliance costs ~10% response; find honest workarounds rather than surrendering all specificity.
20. When you have told the whole story, stop.

## Exact language worth keeping

1. **"A copywriter is a salesperson behind a typewriter."** (Judith Charles) — The identity check for every copy review: is this selling or performing?
2. **"On average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. When you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents out of your dollar."** (Ogilvy) — Justifies spending most of the creative cycle on hooks.
3. **"Copy cannot create desire for a product. It can only take the hopes, dreams, fears, and desires that already exist in the hearts of millions of people, and focus those already-existing desires onto a particular product."** (Eugene Schwartz, quoted by Bly) — Channel, don't manufacture: find what your market already wants.
4. **"Human nature is perpetual. In most respects it is the same today as in the time of Caesar."** (Claude Hopkins) — License to use pre-digital direct-response principles on today's digital platforms.
5. **"We are not in the business of being original. We are in the business of reusing things that work."** (John Francis Tighe) — Defense of proven formulas over novelty.
6. **"The best headlines appeal to people's self-interest, or give news."** (John Caples) — The two-lane test for every hook.
7. Copy words should be **"like the windows in a storefront. The reader should be able to see right through them and see the product."** (Cecil Hoge) — The clarity standard; the writing should be invisible.
8. **"You cannot bore people into buying your product. You can only interest them in buying it."** (Ogilvy) — The counterweight to over-compliance and over-caution.
9. **"Don't tell your prospect about your grass seed, tell him about his lawn."** (Cahners) — Fastest features-to-benefits correction line in existence.
10. **"The letter sells; the brochure tells."** — Division of labor: emotional selling in the primary asset (VSL/ad/sales letter), factual support in secondary assets (landing page, FAQ, spec sheet).
11. **"For each 10 percent closer you get to perfect compliance, your response rates drop by 10 percent."** (Bly) — The compliance/response tradeoff, made explicit.
12. **"Start with the prospect, not the product."** (Don Hauptman) — The BDF entry point for every new angle.

## How to apply this to any brand

The repeatable copy-craft procedure. Run it in order on every new assignment — an ad, an e-mail, a landing page, a VSL, a sales letter. Each step names the frameworks it operationalizes.

**Step 1 — Run BDF on your audience before writing a word.** Write out the market's Beliefs, Desires, and Feelings (Framework 15) and get whoever owns the product to sign off on the write-up. Aim past the intellectual level for the emotional and personal levels, and hunt specifically for the *suppressed feeling* — the thing prospects say to themselves but never out loud (the "Go to hell" letter pattern: the biggest wins come from articulating a feeling, not listing a benefit). Cross-check desires against the 22 copy motivators (Framework 16). If the product sells to more than one segment, write a separate BDF per segment and sell each segment its own benefit — ask buyers what they care about rather than assuming (the water-purifier lesson). Output: one page per segment stating the dominant belief, the dominant desire, and the feeling the copy will name.

**Step 2 — Write 6–8 headline versions and score them on the 4 U's.** Answer Bly's four production questions (Framework 8) — who is the customer, what are the features, which features do competitors lack, why will they buy — then generate at least 6–8 headlines using the eight types (Framework 5) and the 38 swipe approaches (Framework 6) as generators. Score every candidate 1–4 on Urgent / Unique / Ultra-specific / Useful; rewrite until at least three U's score 3+ (Framework 7). Ultra-specificity is the cheapest upgrade — replace category words with numbers, timeframes, and named outcomes. Keep the discards as subheads. In video, the first twenty seconds *is* the headline (Framework 3): draft and score openers exactly the same way.

**Step 3 — Structure the body on the Motivating Sequence.** Attention (single strongest benefit first — never save it) → show the need → satisfy the need → prove it → ask for action (Framework 11). Size the piece with the Copy Length Grid (Framework 17): high emotion + high involvement + high price + unfamiliar + one-step sale → long copy that overcomes every objection; two-step lead-gen → short copy whose only job is the next click. Where skepticism is likely (big claims, expensive products, trained-skeptic audiences), pair the big promise with a secondary promise believable on its own (Framework 14), and calibrate compliance consciously: don't sand every claim to mush; convert banned directness into curiosity, proof, and audience-selecting question headlines (Framework 2, Hard rule 19).

**Step 4 — Readability pass, then the checklists.** Translate every remaining feature through the two-column features→benefits exercise (Framework 10). Enforce the 4 S formula and the 11 rules (Framework 9): you-orientation, 14–16-word average sentences, no jargon under the 95% rule, no warm-up paragraphs, and read it aloud — rewrite anything you would never say to the reader. Finish by running the Copywriter's Checklist and the headline evaluation checklist; ship nothing that fails them. When the whole story is told, stop (Hard rule 20).

### Worked micro-examples

**B2B SaaS — payroll software for restaurants.** BDF: belief — "payroll is a Sunday-night tax on owning a restaurant"; desire — never pay a filing penalty again; feeling — dread of the envelope from the state. Draft headline "Powerful, Flexible Payroll" scores 1-1-1-2 on the 4 U's. Rewrite: "Run payroll for a 30-person restaurant in 11 minutes — and never pay a late-filing penalty again" (3-3-4-4). This is two-step lead-gen → the ad stays short, one idea, curiosity plus audience selection; the demo page carries the full motivating sequence.

**DTC high-ticket consumer — a $2,400 commuter e-bike.** High emotion + high involvement + high price + one-step sale → LONG page copy on the motivating sequence: attention ("Your commute, minus the gym membership and the gas bill") → need (dramatize the 47 minutes lost each way, twice a day) → solution → proof (independent range tests, owner testimonials in their own unpolished words) → action with a reason to act now. Big promise (replace the car for your commute) paired with a secondary promise believable on its own (even if you never commute on it, it replaces the car for every errand under five miles) — the "either way, I can't lose" mechanic.

**Local service — a family dental practice, mailed letter + e-mail.** The letter sells; the brochure tells. Open peer-to-peer with a problem-identification lead: "If your kids' last cleaning felt like an assembly line, this letter is for you" — an audience-selecting question-style headline that names the feeling. Specifics over platitudes ("appointments start on time or the visit is free" beats "we respect your time"), a plain-English guarantee, a P.S. restating the offer (80 percent read it), and one easy response path.
