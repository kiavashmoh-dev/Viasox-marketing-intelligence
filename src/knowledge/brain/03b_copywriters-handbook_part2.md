## Complete craft frameworks

### 1. The 12 types of commercials (Ch. 10)
1. **Demonstration** — show the product working; strongest for comparisons (split screen: their wax vs. ours).
2. **Testimonial** — real users beat paid actors; hidden-camera reactions read as genuine. Celebrities must fit the product and must be identified by name on screen/VO.
3. **Stand-up presenter** ("talking head"/pitchman) — best when the pitch itself is strong enough that it needs no dressing up. Direct-response spots use it because it's measurable and cheap.
4. **Slice of life** — mini-play around the product.
5. **Lifestyle** — sell who the user becomes. Casting note: use actors ~10 years younger and ~10 lbs lighter than the real demographic; people see themselves aspirationally.
6. **Animation** — mainly kids; adult use just replaces live action.
7. **Jingles** — slogan set to music.
8. **Visual as hero** — filmmaking, not selling; Bly has never seen a case study proving it sells.
9. **Humor** — viewers enjoy it but often can't recall the product; "unless you are 99.9 percent sure that you are funny… avoid."
10. **Continuing characters** — fictional recurring character or the owner/founder as the face (Jimmy Dean, the Purdue family; cf. My Pillow's owner-demonstrator spots, cited earlier in the chapter as straight-sell demonstration).
11. **Reason-why** — list the reasons to buy; works better in print than on air.
12. **Emotion** — nostalgia/sentiment; memorable but hard to write. Bly's steer: most writers do better sticking to demonstrations, pitchmen, testimonials — "straight-sell" formats.

Framing tension of the whole chapter: "creative" vs. straight-sell. Ogilvy proved there is **no correlation between liking a commercial and being sold by it**; the serious prospect is an *information seeker*. What works now is "edutainment" — information + entertainment.

### 2. Writing the video/TV spot (Ch. 10)
- TV is pictures first: **if you can't tell what's being sold with the sound off, the spot is weak** — but words must explain the pictures; sight and sound work together.
- Trade off density: heavy words → simple pictures; complex visuals → few words. Viewers can't process both.
- **The first four seconds are the headline** — they decide whether the viewer stays.
- Use motion and sound (sizzle, pour, fizz). Show the label in close-up. Use **supers** (text overlays) to reinforce or add a point ("NOT AVAILABLE IN STORES").
- **Repeat product name + main selling point at least twice** (memory + late joiners).
- One spot = **one main thought**.
- Announce the response ask at the *beginning* ("Get paper and pencil ready…"); use easy-to-remember numbers/URLs.
- Script format: video (pictures) left column, audio (words/SFX) right column. Terms: ANNCR, CU, LS, MS, SFX, TS, VO.
- Standard lengths 10/30/60/120 seconds; DRTV runs 120s or 30-min infomercial.

### 3. Radio (words + sound paint the picture)
Campiti's tips: lock onto salespeople's "keys" (inside info from customer conversations — e.g., the rat-poison "plain brown wrapper" line that fixed a dead campaign); talk benefits; be concise; **repeat key info** (store names 2x, phone at least 2x, URL 1–2x near the end); research the product; know the production house's capabilities. Very short one/two-word sentences set pace.

### 4. Nonbroadcast AV / presentation scripts
Write for the ear, not the eye; crystal clear on first hearing (no referring back); hook in the opening; repeat key selling points several times; spoon-feed — be selective with facts and leave the viewer hungry; 8–10 minutes ideal, 20 max. PowerPoint: visuals only when they beat words; one major visual per slide; **5x5 rule** (≤5 bullets/slide, ≤5 words/bullet); black type on white; be able to present without slides.

### 5. The Agora Model of online marketing (Ch. 11)
The proven engine: (1) Build a site positioning you as the **expert resource in a niche**; (2) offer free content (report/e-zine) in exchange for the e-mail address; (3) drive traffic from many small cheap tests (SEO, e-mail, ads, affiliates, publicity); (4) the bulk of profit comes from **repeat e-mail marketing to the house e-list** at near-zero cost. "Try a lot of different tactics in small and inexpensive tests, throw out the ones that don't work, and do more of the ones that are effective."

Site content = two components (Roger Parker): **information prospects need to know in order to buy** + **information that convinces them to buy** (testimonials, case studies, credentials, books/articles). Every site decision starts from the *business objective* — Bly deliberately does NOT want more traffic; his site exists for instant inquiry fulfillment to qualified prospects.

### 6. Online deliverables taxonomy (know what you're writing)
- **Microsite / long-copy landing page** — sells direct; length of a 4–8-page sales letter.
- **Name squeeze page** — short LP to capture the e-mail (headline + few paragraphs + form).
- **Transaction page** — basically an order form.
- **Long-copy e-mail** (≈2–3-page letter) / **teaser e-mail** (≈half-page, drives to microsite) / **lead-generation e-mail** (drives to free-content LP).
- **Online conversion series** — autoresponder sequence converting inquiry → sale.
- **Online text ad** (50–100 words), **banner**, **pop-under/floater**.

### 7. ClickFunnels page = the Motivating Sequence, stacked vertically
Sections top to bottom follow: (a) get attention, (b) state the problem, (c) show your product is the solution, (d) **prove superiority — spread proof over several descending panels**, one proof element each (testimonials → how it works/what it's made of → awards/press → clinical studies/test results), (e) ask for the order. Copy brief, heavy bullets.

### 8. SEO copywriting rules
2–3 keyword phrases per page; the most important keyword first in the headline (H1); meta description ≤158 characters with keyword at the front; title tag ≤60 characters; descriptive 2–4-word phrases ("Boston cosmetic dentists," not "dentists"); work keywords in naturally — **keyword stuffing is worse than not using them at all**. Multi-page sites outrank one-pagers (more keyword surface). Rotating banners: don't (1–2% ever click; perceived as ads and ignored by 86%; slow load — a 1-second delay loses 7% of visitors; bad on mobile; rotate too fast to read).

### 9. Landing pages (Ch. 12) — the conversion framework
A landing page has ONE job: conversion. Businesses with 10–15 landing pages get **55% more leads**; 40+ pages, **12x** the leads of ≤5.
The 10 keys (full logic in Checklists below): credibility on the first screen → capture non-buyer e-mails (pop-up/pop-under/floater) → testimonials everywhere → bullets in *feature—dash—benefit* format → curiosity/big-promise headline → conversational, human, letter-from-one-person-to-another style → emotional hook in headline+lead → dramatize the problem, then position the action on THIS page as the fastest route to solving it → tie to current events → hammer the guarantee / "free, no obligation."
Structural rules: **no navigation** (respond or don't — no menu to wander off into); marketer's voice clearly identified with expert credentials; tell an engaging story; prove every claim with specifics; big bold colorful CTA buttons plus hyperlinked phrases in-copy; keep content current (outdated content kills credibility).

### 10. E-mail marketing (Ch. 13)
- SUBJECT line = envelope teaser: curiosity + attention without blatant promotion. FROM line options: list owner / your company / you personally / "Team."
- **Deliver a mini-version of the whole message in paragraph one** — offer + response link up top; expanded features/benefits/proof after; **offer and link repeated at the close**.
- ≤3 click-through links; the first two get the majority of clicks.
- 55–60 characters per line; all-caps sparingly; short beats long *in the e-mail itself* because…
- **The copy is split in two**: e-mail (front end) + landing page/microsite (back end). Kathy Henning: online text should be about *half* as long as print. Four quadrants: short e-mail→landing page (lead gen); long e-mail→landing page; long e-mail→microsite (translating a magalog); short e-mail→microsite (long story, impatient readers).
- Tone helpful, informative, specific — never "best"/"quality" puffery. "Information is the gold in cyberspace" (Vitale). Always include opt-out.

### 11. Online conversion series (the follow-up sequence)
Free content → e-mail captured → **3–7 e-mail series**: first 1–2 are "**free touch**" (no ask — thank them, point out the best ideas in the free content, give more value); the rest are "**conversion e-mails**" (ask for the order). Typical cadence: Day 1 free touch, Day 2 free touch, Day 4 conversion (free 30-day trial), Day 7 reminder-solve-your-problem, Day 14 deadline/expiry urgency. Key craft rule: **open every e-mail by acknowledging the free item they requested** — it obligates and warms them. Best offer = genuinely free 30-day trial (card not billed) > "risk-free" (billed but refundable). People online don't buy from strangers; they *will* take free content from strangers — that's the bridge.

### 12. E-zine / newsletter content formula
Best e-zines = practical, actionable how-to tips in bite-size chunks. Ideal article (Benun): be a conduit; harvest questions/problems from real customer interactions; state the problem as intro; give action steps usable *right away*; describe the result/benefit; include verbatim-usable phrases, checklists, forms; link resources; **put your best tip first**. Dual audience: free subscribers (delight + upgrade via a 50–100-word ad per issue + one solo e-mail between issues) and customers (news, upgrades, offers).

### 13. Paid ads by platform (Ch. 14)
- **Banners**: interruption medium — better for awareness than DR; medium rectangle (300×250) has highest CTR; minimal text; benefit ("Get More Traffic"), question, urgency ("For a Limited Time"), "Free," explicit CTA ("Click Here"); relevance to page content drives response.
- **PPC math first**: worth-of-a-click determines max CPC (20 clicks/sale × $1 = $20/order — is margin above that?). List-building variant: average yearly value per name. Keywords: specific > generic ("gold loop earrings" not "jewelry"); "alternative-to" keywords (bid "diamond" to sell cubic zirconium); occasions ("Mother's Day gift"). **Qualify the click**: put price in the ad to repel tire-kickers. Keyword in the headline. Dedicated domain per landing page.
- **Facebook**: the image is responsible for **75–90% of ad performance** — test **10–15 images holding copy constant**. WordStream's 3-part formula for the copy: *what you're offering / how it benefits them / what to do next*. AdEspresso headline guidelines: numbers; urgency; clarity; brevity; benefits; CTA; question; power words. Video: build for mobile (vertical/square); **capture attention immediately; brand/product early; design for silent viewing** (text on screen, imagery that works mute, subtitles). Most effective ad titles: ~4 words; link descriptions ~15 words. 60–70% of viewers of a company's FB video visit the site after.
- **LinkedIn**: B2B lead gen #1; text ads 25-char headline / 75-char description; profile page = mini sales page (results-focused one-liner under the name, bulleted capabilities in caps with one-line descriptions, specific numbers, phone CTA).

### 14. Social media (Ch. 15)
Different post per platform, same destination link (funnel to blog/LP → opt-in). Post structure (FB): ~5-word headline, 2–3 tiny paragraphs (~80 characters total), related image, link. Twitter ≤100 chars ideally + narrow hashtags (#keto beats #diet — narrower = more qualified). Always end with an engagement ask ("What do you think?" "Agree or disagree?"). Business page, not personal. Avoid politics/religion/controversy under a business brand. Check every post on mobile.

### 15. Video scripts (Ch. 16) — the core chapter for video-led funnels
**Types**: 60-second spot; explainer (1–2 min: state problem → offer solution → show product in action); interview; corporate; training; online video (homepage 2–3 min; B2B avg ~4 min; deeper product pages 5–7+).
**Math**: narration ≈ **100–120 words per minute**. Ten-minute script ≈ 1,200 words.
**Governing rule**: "One presentation, one topic" — one product, one idea, one offer.
**Process**: get the creative brief (message? problem solved? where shot? where do they get it?) → build an outline script (scene/visual/audio grid) → write one guiding sentence per scene → draft → **read it aloud, record it, time each section**; cadence must be natural.
**Writing rules**: know the audience; intro gets the message across in 1–2 sentences; concise, no fluff or exotic words; use "you," conversational; **show the problem, then the solution, then the outcome** (life better/easier); end with CTA and exactly how to act.

### 16. Video Sales Letters (VSLs) — the full framework
Format lineage: whiteboard-style text on screen, one or two sentences per slide, read aloud by VO. Typical DR length **15–45 minutes; 2,000–7,000-word script**. Critical structural difference from text sales pages: the viewer **cannot skim or re-read** — she watches linearly — so the script must carry her.
Bly's 9 guidelines:
1. Open with a **pattern interrupt** — a statement that breaks the viewer's normal pattern; surprise, shake them up.
2. Tell an engaging story that sweeps the listener along — Masterson's "**velvet slide**."
3. Keep it simple: **information density ~20% lower** than a text promotion.
4. Short sentences and short words — Bly uses **no word longer than 9 letters**.
5. Short paragraphs (a couple of sentences) so on-screen text reads easily.
6. Insert charts/graphs to dramatize or prove claims — even a few seconds on screen signals the point is well backed up.
7. **State the problem in the lead, but explain the solution within the first minute or two** — wait too long and they click away.
8. **No more than two numbers in a sentence**; round at least one off.
9. Tone: positive, enthusiastic — and authoritative (a voice is reading it).
The kicker: "They're too long! Who would watch?" — **Testing shows repeatedly that VSLs usually generate higher conversion rates than static landing pages.** "Don't let personal preference get in the way" (Beutel).

### 17. Content marketing (Ch. 17)
- Old idea, new name: Koch tray manual (1980), Hopkins's free booklets, Campbell's recipe books (1916). "Bait piece" → "lead magnet."
- **Adding a lead magnet to a B2B lead-gen campaign can double inquiries or more.** For B2C it's usually a bonus gift with purchase.
- **The 4 levels of content** (climb as high as you can): *why* (argument only) → *what* (steps named) → *how* (actionable instruction) → **done-for-you** (templates, scripts, models the reader can lift). Weak writers stop at why/what.
- **The 4 modes of absorption**: reading, listening, watching, doing — produce content across all four because you don't know each prospect's preference.
- **Rename the white paper** to raise perceived value: "special report," "guide," "executive briefing," "consumer awareness guide," "buyer's/selection guide," "manual," "free booklet." Put a dollar price on the cover.
- Lead-with-offer rule of thumb: **exceptional free content → lead with the offer**; average content → lead with the prospect's problem, product as solution; superior product → sell the product, close with the lead magnet as response sweetener.
- **The BDF formula** (Masterson) replaces expensive research: What do prospects **Believe**? **Desire**? **Feel**? Case study: IT soft-skills seminar flyer went from <0.5% to 3% response (6:1) after BDF analysis produced the headline "IMPORTANT NEWS FOR ANY IT PROFESSIONAL WHO HAS EVER FELT LIKE TELLING AN END USER, 'GO TO HELL.'" 30-to-1 ROI, zero research spend.
- **The Schwartz limit** — what content marketing cannot do: "Copy cannot create desire for a product. It can only take the hopes, dreams, fears, and desires that already exist… and focus those already-existing desires onto a particular product… not to create this mass desire — but to channel and direct it." Your two levers: (a) dramatize severity/urgency of the problem, (b) educate on the best solution.
- **Case studies** = product success stories, 800–1,500 words, magazine-feature style, soft sell. Believable because the praise comes from a user with no financial motive; prospects identify with peers. Interview craft: wring out **numbers** ("Did it reduce energy more than 10 percent?"); use the "**So are you saying…**" technique to get quotable phrasing the subject signs off on; quotes double as testimonials; get signed releases and keep them; recruit case-study subjects as reference accounts.
- Blogs: two-way medium; strong statement + "What do you think?"; ~1,000 words/post, ≥1/week; big SEO lift.

### 18. Reviewing and approving copy (Ch. 18) — how to be a good client of your own copy
- **The 25-50-25 principle of revisions**: ~25% of client changes improve copy, 25% weaken it, 50% neutral. Make the good 75% without quibble; argue the harmful 25% once, then acquiesce pleasantly.
- Critique specifically, in writing, factually — "not enough pizzazz" is useless; "our process is 20% more efficient; that should be in the headline" is usable.
- **≤4 reviewers ideal, 6 max** — "the quality of marketing copy is inversely proportional to the number of people who review it." (Koch's moose: "a cow designed by committee.")
- **Read copy as a customer, not an editor** — would this get MY attention, hold me, move me to respond?
- No opinion polls of friends/family — amateurs judge aesthetics, not selling power.
- Compliance (supplements etc.): can't claim cure; "supports," better "may help support." One marketer's rule: "For each 10 percent your copy gets closer to strict 100 percent compliance, your response rate drops about 10 percent."
- Publish copy guidelines up front; base them on tested data (e.g., client learned 8–12-word headlines pull best in his half-page ads).

### 19. Design for response (Ch. 19)
- Copywriter supplies a **copywriter's rough** — crude sketch positioning headline, visual, CTA, response device. The layout's job is to make copy easy to read. Casual browsers don't read long copy; **buyers do** — and without detail they don't buy.
- **Basic A layout**: big picture top, headline under it, 2–3 columns of body copy, logo bottom right. Unfashionable, effective.
- One focal point; eye pulled headline → visual → body → logo via subheads and bullets. On a web page the eye goes to the **upper right of the home page first — put a prominent CTA there**.
- Black type on white; never reverse type, tints, or type over images. Color for emphasis only, sparingly.
- Photos beat drawings (more believable). Best photos demonstrate a benefit or have story appeal (arouse curiosity so the reader enters the copy).
- Simple layouts win; a plain-Jane all-copy black-and-white ad sold $5M+ of a language course. "The Bible contains many thousands of words and not a single picture."

## Checklists

**TV spot checklist (Ch. 10):** selling message survives sound-off · words explain pictures · dense words = simple pictures (and vice versa) · would it stop someone heading to the fridge? · budget-aware format (presenter/demo are cheapest) · first 4 seconds grab · show the label in CU · motion · sound · supers · name + main point ≥2x · show product in use · announce response ask early · identify celebrities · one main thought.

**Radio checklist (Campiti):** salespeople's "keys" · customer-feedback selling points · benefits · short sentences · repeat key info (stores 2x, phone 2x+, URL 1–2x late) · know the product · know the production resources.

**AV/presentation checklist:** write for the ear · vivid, precise spoken words · clear on first hearing · research · repeat key points · hook the opening · active verbs · spoon-feed selective facts, leave them hungry · words paint pictures complementing visuals · concise, no complex sentences. PowerPoint: keywords not sentences · no decorative clip art · plain backgrounds · 5x5 rule · mix word slides with image slides (≤3 images, ideally 1).

**Why no rotating banners:** 1–2% click · perceived as ads, 86% don't recall · heavy files slow load (1s delay = −7% visitors) · bad on mobile · rotate too fast to read.

**10 keys to landing-page conversion:** 1 credibility early (logo/seal/testimonial/mission on first screen) · 2 capture non-buyer e-mails (pop-up, pop-under, floater) · 3 lots of testimonials (video with click-to-play; customers' own words, never ghostwritten puffery) · 4 lots of bullets, feature—dash—benefit · 5 curiosity/big-promise headline · 6 conversational, one-human-to-another · 7 emotional hook in headline + lead; stress FREE offer for lead gen · 8 solve the reader's problem — this page's action is the fastest route · 9 timely/current copy · 10 stress guarantee / free / no obligation.

**Additional landing-page guidelines:** big compelling benefit in headline · identified, credentialed voice · engaging story · prove claims with specifics · uncluttered layout · CTA button → cart/transaction page · big bold colorful buttons + hyperlinked phrases · optional toll-free number · accurate, current content · graphics serve readability (contrast!).

**7 traffic drivers:** paid search · affiliates · co-registration · banners (test conservatively) · e-mail to rented opt-in lists (watch acquisition cost/name) · e-zine ads (higher placement = higher response) · viral forwarding (requires a real offer).

**15 e-mail tips (condensed):** teaser-style SUBJECT · FROM line strategy (owner/company/you/Team) · "FREE" usually lifts despite filters · killer lead sentence · mini-version of message in ¶1 with link · expanded proof after · offer+link at open AND close · ≤3 links (first two get most clicks) · 55–60-char lines · caps sparingly · shorter than print · key points on first screen · helpful not hypey, specific not "quality" · opt-out statement · whitelist reminder line at top.

**Deliverability (condensed):** reverse DNS · SPF · one message per connection · limit send rate · accept bounces · valid HTML · no scripts · learn content-filter basics · monitor bounce/opens by ISP · monitor spam complaints against benchmark. Then: Monitor / Analyze / Resolve / Optimize.

**Ideal e-zine article (Benun):** be a conduit · harvest real customer questions · distill the lesson to a tip · state the problem as intro · give action steps · describe the result/benefit · include verbatim-usable material (phrases, boilerplate, checklists, forms) · link resources · best tip first.

**Facebook ad copy:** WordStream — what you're offering / how it benefits them / what to do next. AdEspresso headlines — numbers · urgency · clarity · brevity · benefits · CTA · question · power words. Video — mobile-first vertical/square · instant capture · brand early · silent-viewing design · on-screen text · subtitles.

**Video script rules:** know the audience · 1–2-sentence intro that lands the message · 100–120 wpm, ≤2 pages · concise, simple words · "you," conversational · problem → solution shown · outcome (life better/easier) · CTA with exact next step. Afterward: read aloud, record, time.

**VSL checklist:** pattern-interrupt open · velvet-slide story · info density −20% vs. text · ≤9-letter words, short sentences · 2-sentence paragraphs · charts/graphs as proof · solution surfaced in first 1–2 minutes · ≤2 numbers/sentence, round one · positive + authoritative tone.

**7 content mistakes:** mediocre writing · Google-goulash research · stopping at why/what instead of how/done-for-you · no SME interviews · writer doesn't understand the subject · uncredited sources · unsupported opinion (prove points, don't just make them).

**9-step white paper campaign:** target market → problem ID ("what keeps them up at night?") → solution ID (product strengths vs. competition) → content (narrow topic, tailored to the buyer, moves them to the next buying step, outline vetted by SMEs) → media (the more targeted the list, the higher the response — the Apprentice bridal-shop lesson: targeted e-mail beat street flyers 12:1) → tactics (governed by cost + audience access) → schedule (tasks, owners, dates) → budget (creation + fulfillment + paid traffic) → metrics (CTR → download conversion → inquiries → qualified prospects → % purchased).

**Case study outline:** who is the customer · what was the problem and how did it hurt · what solutions did they reject and why · why they chose ours · implementation (incl. problems solved) · how/where they use it · results and benefits (numbers!) · would they recommend it and why.

**Copy review rules:** specific written criticisms · ≤4 approvers (6 max) · compliance awareness · civility ("What I like about this is… If it were mine to do…") · let writers write — specify changes, don't rewrite · no opinion polls · read as a customer · publish tested guidelines.

**Lori Haller's 10 design tips:** copy is KING · readability makes or breaks the campaign · know the reader deeply · fonts sized for the audience (older eyes need bigger type, narrower columns) · current color trends · you can force the reading path · get in their shoes/pain · read the testimonials · order/cart pages simple — reader must not feel tricked · writer + designer as one tight strategic unit.

## Hard rules (numbered)

1. If you can't tell what's being sold with the sound off, the video isn't strong enough.
2. The first four seconds of a video are the headline; win them or lose the viewer.
3. One video = one main thought, one product, one offer.
4. Repeat product name + main selling point at least twice per spot.
5. Script narration at 100–120 words per minute; time it by reading aloud.
6. VSL: state the problem in the lead, deliver the solution within the first 1–2 minutes.
7. VSL: no word over 9 letters; no more than two numbers per sentence (round one off).
8. VSL info density ≈ 20% below the equivalent text promotion.
9. Never judge VSLs (or any format) by personal taste — testing shows they outconvert static pages.
10. Landing pages carry no navigation: respond or leave, nothing in between.
11. Put a credibility element on the first screen before asking for anything.
12. Bullets in feature—dash—benefit format; sell the transformation, not the spec.
13. E-mail: whole story (offer + link) in the first paragraph; repeat at the close; ≤3 links.
14. Split the persuasion between e-mail (front end) and landing page (back end); online text ≈ half print length.
15. Conversion series: 3–7 e-mails; first 1–2 free touch, no ask; always open by referencing the free item they requested.
16. Copy cannot create desire — it can only channel desire that already exists (Schwartz). Pick angles accordingly.
17. Lead with the offer only if the free content is exceptional; otherwise lead with the problem.
18. Name your lead magnet for value ("special report," "guide"), never "white paper."
19. Content should climb the ladder: why → what → how → done-for-you. Ship "how" or higher.
20. Prove every claim with specifics — facts, figures, testimonials, charts; never "best" or "quality."
21. In case studies and testimonials, wring out numbers and use the subject's own words ("So are you saying…?" + sign-off + signed release).
22. Facebook: creative (image/video) drives 75–90% of results — test 10–15 visuals with copy held constant.
23. Design videos for silent viewing: on-screen text, subtitles, visuals that carry meaning mute.
24. Maximum 4–6 copy approvers; committees write moose.
25. Black type, white background, simple layout, photos over drawings; readability beats style every time.
26. Read copy as the customer, never as the editor.

## Exact language worth keeping

1. **"You can't bore people into buying your product."** — Ogilvy. The counterweight to pure information; earn attention, then inform.
2. **"There is no correlation between a person liking a commercial and being sold by it."** — the Ogilvy-era finding Bly leans on. Kills the "entertaining ad" trap before it starts.
3. **"The first four seconds of a commercial are like the headline of a print ad."** — the hook budget for every ad and VSL open.
4. **"Copy cannot create desire for a product… This is the copywriter's task: not to create this mass desire — but to channel and direct it."** — Eugene Schwartz, quoted in Ch. 17. The strategic law underneath angle selection.
5. **"The velvet slide."** — Masterson's name for a story that sweeps the listener down the VSL with no exit points.
6. **"Don't let personal preference get in the way."** — Peter Beutel, on marketers who "hate long VSLs" that outconvert everything.
7. **"Information is the gold in cyberspace."** — Joe Vitale. The tone rule for e-mail and content: helpful, specific, educational — never hype.
8. **"We are drowning in information but starved for knowledge."** — Rutherford Rogers. Why a genuinely useful lead magnet still cuts through.
9. **"IMPORTANT NEWS FOR ANY IT PROFESSIONAL WHO HAS EVER FELT LIKE TELLING AN END USER, 'GO TO HELL.'"** — the BDF case-study headline (6:1 lift). Template for emotion-first, peer-voiced headlines aimed at a profession's private frustration.
10. **"The quality of marketing copy is inversely proportional to the number of people who review it."** — Bly's law of committees ("a moose is a cow designed by committee" — David Koch).
11. **"For each 10 percent your copy gets closer to strict 100 percent compliance, your response rate drops about 10 percent."** — the compliance/response trade-off, stated plainly. Navigate it consciously (critical in regulated categories: health, supplements, finance).
12. **"Copy is KING. ALWAYS. PERIOD."** — Lori Haller, a designer saying it. Settles the copy-vs-design argument in one line.

## How to apply this to any brand

The second half of the Handbook assembles into one repeatable build for a **video-led funnel**: VSL → dedicated landing page per angle → conversion e-mail series → social/paid ad creative feeding the top. Run the procedure below for any product or service; the micro-examples show it flexing across industries.

### The procedure

1. **Run BDF on every angle before writing a single script (§17).** Each candidate angle is a different Belief–Desire–Feeling stack: what does this segment already *believe* (about the category, about vendors like you, about "solutions like this"), what do they privately *desire*, and what do they *feel* when the problem bites? The Schwartz limit (quote #4) says copy cannot create desire — each angle must channel a desire the prospect already has, and the ad's only two levers are (a) dramatizing the problem's severity/urgency and (b) educating on the best solution. An angle that requires manufacturing a new desire is dead on arrival; kill it here, before any production spend.

2. **Build the VSL to Bly's spec, not to intuition (§16).** Pattern-interrupt cold open aimed at the audience's *private* frustration (the BDF headline model: what would this prospect never say out loud?); problem stated in the lead but the solution surfaced inside the first two minutes; a velvet-slide story — usually the founder's or a customer's arc; ≤9-letter words, 2-sentence beats, ~100–120 wpm (so a 20-minute VSL ≈ 2,000–2,400 words); one or two simple charts to visually "prove" the central claims; positive, authoritative tone. And trust the format: testing says long VSLs outconvert static pages — resist the urge to cut it because "our buyers are busy."

3. **One dedicated landing page per angle, built on the Ch. 12 conversion frame (§9, §7).** Zero navigation; first screen = credibility (credentials, a qualifier line naming exactly who it's for, one strong peer testimonial); an emotional-hook headline written per angle; feature—dash—benefit bullets on the mechanics; proof stacked in descending panels (testimonials → how it works → results/awards/press → studies or data); a **low-friction CTA framed as free and no-obligation** — free trial, free sample, free quote, free consult, free audit — whatever the lowest-commitment first step is in your sales model, never a cold "buy now" for a considered purchase. Multiple angles × dedicated pages also cashes in Bly's stat that 10–15 landing pages produce 55% more leads.

4. **Social/paid creative on the creative-first math (§13).** The visual does 75–90% of the work, so hold copy roughly constant and rotate 10–15 hooks/visuals per angle. The stand-up presenter (founder or real customer on camera, straight sell) is Bly's cheapest and most measurable format — no production gloss needed. Every video: vertical/square, hook in the first 4 seconds, brand/product early, captions and on-screen text for silent viewing; primary copy in the WordStream frame (what it is / what it does for you / what to do next); ~4-word headline.

5. **Install a conversion e-mail series behind the funnel (§11).** For prospects who watch the VSL or take the lead magnet but don't act: 3–7 e-mails; first 1–2 free touch (open by referencing the item they requested, pull out its best insight, no ask); then conversion e-mails driving back to the CTA page; close with a genuine deadline. Name the lead magnet for value ("guide," "special report," never "white paper") and pitch it at the how/done-for-you content level.

6. **Mint case studies as you sell (§17).** Use the 8-question outline and the "So are you saying…" interview technique; wring out numbers; get signed releases and keep them. Customer success stories with numbers are the single most believable asset a considered-purchase offer can put in front of skeptical prospects — and every quote doubles as ad testimonial copy.

### Worked micro-examples

**B2B SaaS — project-management tool for agencies.** BDF: agency owners *believe* "another tool = another thing my team ignores," *desire* margin and fewer fire drills, *feel* dread before Monday status calls. The angle channels the existing desire (margin recovery), not an invented one. VSL ~12 min ≈ 1,300 words: pattern interrupt — "The average agency writes off 11% of hours it actually worked" — solution named by minute two; one chart of write-offs before/after. Two angles (margin recovery vs. client retention) → two dedicated LPs, no nav, client-logo credibility bar on the first screen, CTA = genuinely free 30-day trial with card not billed (Bly's best offer). Series: Day 1–2 free touch on the "Billable Leakage Guide," Day 4 trial ask, Day 7 solve-your-problem, Day 14 expiry. Creative: founder talking head, 10–15 visual hooks per angle, copy constant.

**DTC supplement — sleep formula.** The compliance rule bites here: "supports," better "may help support" — and every 10% closer to strict compliance ≈ −10% response, so decide the trade-off consciously, in writing, before creative starts. Emotional-hook headline built off the BDF *feeling* (staring at the ceiling at 3 a.m.), not the ingredient list. VSL 15–20 min, story-led, two charts (sleep-onset data, rounded numbers, ≤2 per sentence). LP: testimonials everywhere in customers' own words, feature—dash—benefit bullet per ingredient, guarantee hammered; capture non-buyer e-mails with an exit offer. CTA = risk-reversed first bottle. Creative plan honors the 75–90% rule: 10–15 image/video tests before any copy test.

**High-ticket local service — kitchen remodeling, $40k average job.** Nobody buys a remodel from an e-mail, so the funnel's only job is a booked design consultation — every CTA is the free, no-obligation consult (low-friction framing). VSL = 6–8 min homeowner-story video on the LP; presenter format = the owner on camera walking a finished kitchen (demonstration + testimonial hybrid, Bly's straight-sell steer). Lead magnet renamed for value — "The Kitchen Remodel Cost & Timeline Guide" with a dollar price on the cover — pitched at done-for-you level (budget worksheet inside). Conversion series closes on a real deadline (this quarter's remaining install slots). Case studies = before/after with the job's actual numbers and the homeowner's own words, signed release on file.
