## Core thesis

Media buying is a solved, compressed skill — targeting is automated, broad-only is right for 99% of accounts, and the old hacks don't arbitrage anymore. **The arbitrage in 2026–27 is creative**, because most advertisers are bad at it. The winning operating model is a *flywheel*: research/ideate concepts → brief → produce at volume above a quality bar → launch into a concept-segmented structure → analyze at the right level → replicate winners and iterate → feed learnings back into ideation. Underneath it all sits **portfolio management**: every ad will fatigue and every ad has a finite total spend capacity, so the job is to keep as many ads as possible holding as much spend as possible, never over-leveraging into one or two winners. Creative is a **revenue driver, not a cost center**; Meta is merely the distribution layer.

## The complete system

### 1. Why creative is the lever (and the three mistakes)

Portfolio model: at $1,000/day, three ads blended at 4x ROAS beats one ad at 4x every time — any one can fatigue and the others absorb spend. Businesses that build eight figures on 2–3 hero creatives and stop producing eventually decline 40% YoY and are often unrecoverable ("you should have fixed that six to eight months ago").

Three team-killing mistakes:
1. **Confusing activity for strategy** — e.g., accounts launching 2,000+ new ads/month, unable to crack $250k/month spend, unprofitable. Volume without a quality bar and concept strategy is junk volume.
2. **Misdiagnosing the bottleneck** — creative bottlenecks include quality, format diversification, insufficient assets at specific awareness stages/funnel levels, missing partnership ads. Fixing the wrong one moves nothing.
3. **Creative as a cost center** — the legacy mindset that platform spend drives revenue and production is a cost to minimize. Backwards: creative drives revenue (also organically, for free); platforms distribute it.

### 2. How the algorithm works in 2026 (Andromeda)

- **The creative you load IS the targeting.** Pre-Andromeda you picked audience then attached ads; post-Andromeda you load ads with no targeting, and Meta reads the transcript (every ad is auto-transcribed), copy, visuals, and historical conversion data to find the audience. Ads that "call out everyone" confuse the system; ads whose hook names an irrelevant persona get pushed to the wrong audience.
- **Andromeda bundling & creative similarity score**: similar creatives (same shoot, slight script variants, same audience) get bundled and served to the same pool → frequency up, reach down, ROAS down. Diverse creatives each carve their own audience → frequency low, more spend unlocked. A "creative similarity score" exists (ask your Meta rep); higher = worse.
- **Sequencing**: one impression rarely converts. Per a Meta-published finding, **after two impressions of an identical creative, purchase probability declines precipitously** (second view can actually be equal/slightly higher; third drops, fourth collapses toward zero). Rotating in a *novel* creative as the 3rd/4th impression keeps purchase probability high. Meta actively optimizes the *sequence* of creatives shown per user (ad 1 → ad 2 → ad 3 → purchase).
- **Diagnostic**: cold ads should almost never show frequency above 2 over 30–60 days. Frequency >2 on cold = fatigue/diversity red flag.
- **Attribution trap**: Meta reports last-click, but optimizes on multi-click-and-view. The "closer" ad in a sequence shows 6x ROAS while the top-of-funnel opener shows 1.4x — turning off the opener destroys the sequence. **Never turn ads off inside an ad set that's hitting KPI.**
- **Reliability ladder**: ad-level ROAS = unreliable (sequencing + last click); ad-set level = trustworthy if concepts are properly siloed (some cross-ad-set serving still occurs); campaign level on **7-day click with existing customers excluded** = highly reliable, generally congruent with the P&L. Make decisions at ad set/campaign level.
- Four practical implications: (1) concept diversity, (2) format variety (Meta won't serve formats a user never engages — e.g., he personally never receives UGC or DPAs), (3) hook quality (the hook steers targeting), (4) volume — **mean spend capacity per ad unit is ~$700–$1,500**, so you need many ads to hold scale.

### 3. Concept architecture: persona → angle → offer (→ format)

**A concept = persona + angle + offer.** Diversity requires changing at least one variable. Post-Andromeda, **format** is a fourth (weakest) diversity lever.

**Personas** are archetypes defined by *problems, desires, and buying triggers* — not demographics. Process: manually build 30–40 genuinely specific personas (avatars → problems solved → differences in pain points, language, and required proof), then feed the full list plus deep business context to an LLM for 20–30 more; keep the best 4–5, iterate again. LLMs "only output the mean" — thin input yields the same average personas your competitors get.
- Broad persona = death. "Women 25–45" / "busy professional" / "health-conscious parent" produce forgettable creative. Instead: *"a consultant who flies twice a week and needs a carry-on wardrobe that works for boardrooms and bars"*; *"a mom who's tried six different supplements because her toddler won't eat vegetables."* Five ads for the hyper-specific persona beat five for the broad one; truly broad unaware ads are a $50M+-brand skill.

**Angles** are the specific argument. Tactical components to assemble into a script:
- **Problem agitation** — lead with pain, make the viewer feel seen before introducing solution (only works on problem-aware audiences).
- **Contrarian truth** — "Everything you've been told about X is wrong"; creates cognitive friction; works at all awareness levels, organic and paid.
- **Social proofing** — usually stacked toward the back of the ad.
- **Domain authority** — expertise *in the domain* (a marathon runner for running products; not a lab coat everywhere).
- **Curiosity gap** — open an information gap, delay the answer to the end to hold viewers through the ad.
- **Comparison / objection handling** — "you've tried X, Y, Z; here's why they failed and this works."
- **Transformation (before/after)** — best-performing element ever, least compliant; must be snuck past Meta's flags.

Worked example concept: persona = casual male runner training for a sub-4-hour marathon, gym-goer, follows named influencers, ~$70k income (income informs offer framing). Angle = science-backed (Huberman-adjacent), creatine improves recovery *for the long run*, enabling a 5-minute marathon improvement. Offer = creatine gummies, buy-2-get-1. Formats: UGC, founder talking head, educational high-fi, carousel (VSL excluded — demographic too young). Then mix-and-match: swap persona to women marathoners; tweak angle to first-5K runners — each is a new concept.

**Testing priority order** (most people run this exactly backwards):
1. **Angle** — highest priority. If people who've bought before aren't converting, it's a message/argument problem. "Bad creative is normally a function of the angle and the scripting."
2. **Offer** — arguably most important but least flexible: the product is the product. Value-prop tests (bundles, gift-with-purchase, tiered % off, subscription) hit a wall after ~7 variations; avoid deep-discount dependence.
3. **Persona** — the breadth/volume unlock once angles work.
4. **Format** — weakest diversity lever; "UGC as a concept doesn't work. It is the script, the persona, the angle, the offer contained within the format that works."

**Structural implication**: segment **ad sets by concept**. Targeting lives at the ad set level; mixed concepts in one ad set confuse targeting *and* make results unlearnable (which concept won?).

### 4. Hooks

The hook is **a play toward relevancy, not a trick** — a promise that what follows is relevant to the viewer. Baiting/meme hooks inflate hook rate without conversions. Three layers: **visual, audio, copy overlay** (incl. primary text) — changing any is a hook change. **80% of viewers never watch past the hook**, so it's the highest-leverage seconds of any video. Resource allocation: 2 bodies × 15 hooks beats 2 hooks × 5 bodies (assuming scripting is good) — the available performance delta is bigger in the hook.

**Hook grading rubric** (need only a couple scored well, not all five):
1. **Clarity** — can a stranger understand it in 3 seconds?
2. **Relevance** — agitate the problem; **persona callouts are overrated** ("Agency owners, listen up" → skip; the specific problem → watch).
3. **Novelty** — white space / "purple ocean" (novel positioning with proven product-market fit; between red-ocean sameness and risky blue-ocean). Ask: has this hook been done a million times?
4. **Specificity** — numbers, names, quantified outcomes ("67% of people got this result").
5. **Credibility** — usually visual: a famous or contextually authoritative person on screen.

**Nine named hook types** (translate, don't copy):
1. **Problem agitation** — "Are you struggling with back pain from desk work?" (problem-aware only; not scalable above that stage).
2. **Contrarian truth** — "Everything you've been told about protein timing is wrong."
3. **Specific proof** — "I lost 12 kilos in 90 days without giving up pasta."
4. **Curiosity gap** — "What the top 1% of brands know about Meta ads that you don't" (his average view time with it: 45+ seconds).
5. **Truth bomb** — lead with the uncomfortable truth about price/ingredients/industry: "This product costs $120. Yeah, it's super expensive, but it's our best seller. Here's why." Exceptional for premium/high-AOV where price is the main objection.
6. **Psychological confrontation** — a friend calling you out: "You know that drawer full of products you never use? That's the problem."
7. **Sensory/texture (ASMR)** — 2–3 wordless seconds of fabric handled, liquid poured, package opened; crushing in fashion right now.
8. **Founder's letter** — origin story with *real stakes*: "I remortgaged my house and put $300,000 down to order my first batch. Here's what's happened since." Risk builds tension; can't be faked.
9. **Social proof** — "90% of customers saw results in 30 days."

**The bridge** (hook→body, body→CTA) is where good ads die. Never step-change product awareness ("…training for a marathon? Well, creatine from [brand] is your solution" — hook fine, bridge killed it). Blend the sell in gradually; move viewers through the awareness stages (unaware → problem aware → solution aware → product aware → convert) *inside* the ad — VSLs excel because they have room to teach. Counter-intuitive rule: **introduce the product as late as possible.** Retention-curve logic ("80% drop by 90s, so pitch before 90s") is wrong — later product introduction means the remaining viewers are pre-framed, qualified, high-intent, and total purchase volume is higher despite fewer eyeballs. Also: avoid "contrast negation" scripting tics ("it's not X, it's Y") — the tell of an AI-written script.

**Hook testing post-Andromeda still works**: a new hook = a new creative ID, reaching a novel audience (with partial overlap). It's nearly free — record 6 hook takes per shoot; rotate 50 new hooks onto a fatigued $50–100k winner and get another $100k of spend at the same efficiency from ~1 hour of shooting. Highest-leverage iteration in existence.

### 5. Formats

Five questions, in order: (1) What's the concept? Concept first — **never start with format and work backwards.** (2) Does the concept require education? → longer-form. (3) Can the value prop live in a single frame? → statics/carousels. (4) Does the product need a demo? (5) What awareness stage is this asset for?

- **Statics**: two strategic uses — cheap fast testing of angles/concepts (winners graduate into video), and standalone scale. Run the **cost-of-production vs revenue-per-ad math**: average revenue per ad ≈ $2,000 for most accounts; delineate to contribution margin per ad (e.g., $350) vs cost to produce (e.g., $400 = broken equation). Example split: images $50 cost/$300 CM vs videos $500 cost/$400 CM → double down on images even though videos "perform better." People do $200k/day in revenue on AI images alone. "Statics are bottom-of-funnel" is a skill issue — statics with $500k lifetime spend exist.
- **Long-form-copy static image ads** crush for 50+ Facebook-feed demographics: a story pulls unaware readers through every awareness stage. Requires real copywriting skill (hence the four books).
- **Tweet-format / whitelisted statics** (IM-8 breakdown): ad runs through a doctor's personal profile, looks like an organic repost, problem-agitates on a huge symptom list (wide TAM), educates (symptom → deficiency → vitamin), then sells in the copy. Organic feel = the weapon.
- **Advertorial/listicle statics** ("best hair-growth products of 2025 and the ones to avoid") → education-based landing page ("dermatologist reviews") → quiz funnel → conversion; competitors listed but unclickable or sent to Amazon affiliate links. Only works with an education-heavy LP or primary text.
- **VSLs**: the top format for older demographics in 2026; pull viewers through all awareness stages; can be ~90% AI-produced end-to-end and some hold hundreds of thousands in spend. Breakdown patterns: persona-callout open on symptoms, objection-handle the conventional fix ("treating the symptom, not the cause"), problem-educate, open curiosity gaps, prescribe the problem to the viewer with prevalence stats ("40% of people have it — you might be among them"), then late product intro with mechanism, clinical proof, social proof.
- **The 25M-view VSL** (running 3–4 years): news-anchor split-screen + celebrity trainer, stacked contrarian takes for the first 28 seconds, curiosity gap held throughout, education into a proprietary system. The influencer-profile testosterone ad: product introduced at ~90 seconds, fast cuts, format switching mid-ad, native to platform — why it scales.
- **DPAs (dynamic product ads)**: the "death spiral" format. Attributed ROAS looks great but collapses once you exclude existing customers and apply **incremental attribution** (Columns → Compare attribution settings → Incremental). Mechanism: Meta holds out ~10% of the target pool; if control converts at 1% and treated at 2%, true lift is 1% — a reported 5x ROAS is really 2.5x. Brands defund top-of-funnel to feed the "high-ROAS" DPA, the funnel starves, everything topples. $1k/day cold on DPA: fine. $50k/day: never seen it.
- **Funnel allocation**: 70–90% top of funnel, ~20% middle, 5–10% bottom — expressed today through **creative production volume** (what % of assets you make per awareness stage), not campaign budget lines, because campaign structure should be consolidated.
- **Partnership (collaborator) ads**: the most under-utilized format of 2026 (arbitrage closing). Run through the creator's handle (Meta split-tests brand/creator/both label combos); targeting leverages *both* audiences — the influencer's audience data helps find cold prospects; feels organic. Some accounts run up to 40% of total spend through them. Not running them = competitive disadvantage.

### 6. Testing framework & account structure

**Testing cycle**: hypothesis (define the variable being tested — usually one of the four concept components) → build **10+ ads per concept** (typical account hit rate ≈ 5%, so ~20 ads to *expect* one winner; 1–3 ads per concept is not a test) → launch → analyze against the hypothesis (expected spend, conversions, ROI vs actual) → scale winners → iterate. The loop accelerates with spend.

**Structure principle**: *"The complexity of the ad account should be a product of the complexity of the business."* A $10k/month single-product startup needs a dead-simple account; an $800M retailer with 50 new SKUs/week needs complexity that mirrors the commercial reality. Default small/mid-size template:
- **Testing campaign** (ABO or CBO — see risk below); **ad sets = concepts**; minimum 3 ads per ad set, upper bound 50–100+ (budget-dependent; think in lower limits).
- **Adding ads to a winning ad set resets learning/sequencing — don't.** New ads for a winning concept go into a *new* ad set ("Concept 1 — Shoot 2"). If the ad set is missing KPI, do whatever you want to it — the sequencing isn't working anyway.
- **Scaling a winner**: raise the budget; **never turn a winner off** to "relaunch it in a scaling campaign" (rookie mistake). Optionally duplicate the *post IDs* into a scaling campaign (works in many accounts, not all — keep if it works, kill if not).
- **Third campaign**: retargeting on existing customers, so existing customers can be excluded from the cold campaigns. Optionally a 90-day-website-visitor ad set if the purchase journey is long and engaged frequency is needed.

**Metric hierarchy (order of reliability for judging creative)**:
1. **Amount spent** — the best proxy. Meta funnels budget to ads doing sequencing work even when their last-click ROAS looks bad. An ad with double the spend and a 1.7x beats a 2.2x that can't attract spend.
2. **ROAS / CPA** — only on 7-day click or incremental attribution.
3. **CPC / CTR** — leading indicators only; usable at the fringes (e.g., if no ad above a $3 CPC has ever been profitable, kill new ads crossing it early).
4. **Hook rate** (% past 3 seconds) and **hold rate** (% past ~15 seconds) — video-only, *not* correlated with conversion; purely diagnostic for iteration (bad hook rate → swap hooks; bad hold → rebuild the bridge).

**ABO vs CBO = a risk decision.** CBO obeys Pareto — and Pareto compounds: 20% of ads take 80% of spend, and 20% of *that* 20% takes 80% of *that* 80%, so **~4% of ads end up holding ~64% of spend/revenue**. CBO = higher ROI, higher concentration risk (terrible for portfolio management and for controlling sell-through across a product range). ABO = slightly lower ROI, distributed risk. His default: ABO steady-state; flip to CBO temporarily when you need a short-term efficiency spike.

**The 90/10 problem**: legacy brands (campaign-shoot DNA) run ~90% desire-led / 10% pain-led creative. The best performance assets sit on pain. Rebalance to 60/40, best brands ~70/30 pain:desire (think performance assets vs branding assets).

**Bottom-of-funnel/retargeting creative = objection handling, mapped to why the ~95% didn't buy** (at 5% site CVR). Model the objection mix, then build creative per objection: ~20% price → discounts, value anchoring, cost-per-use angles ("the price of a coffee, with the same caffeine" — these crush BOF); ~20% trust → social proof volume, five-star reviews, UGC testimonials; ~20% quality doubt → UGC in use, before/afters, demos; ~20% not-ready-yet → stay top-of-mind with education/lifestyle. Your BOF creative mix should mirror the objection mix.

### 7. Volume, quality bar, and the financial model

**Leverage hierarchy**: volume > concept quality > hook strength > editing quality > UGC talent > storytelling. Volume creates quality — but only above a bar.

**The 7/10 rule**: no one — not even ten elite creative strategists — can predict which of five objectively good ads will win. Stop trying to pick winners. Instead, internalize what a **7/10 ad** looks like (good concept + good hook + format matched to awareness stage), ensure everything clears the bar, and maximize volume above it. Junk volume below the bar (the 2,000-ads/month accounts) fails regardless of quantity.

**Volume formulas**:
- Simple: **1 new ad per $1,000 in monthly spend** ($30k/mo → 30 ads/mo; $50k → 50).
- Better (back-propagation): mean spend per ad (e.g., 500 ads / $500k = $1,000) × average ROAS (7-day click; e.g., 3x) = expected revenue per ad launched ($3,000). Divide the new-customer revenue target by it: $300k target ÷ $3k = **100 creatives/month**. Flex creative volume with the revenue forecast through the year. (Caveats the simple model misses: time-to-spend per ad, and winner/loser distribution — forecast them separately.)

**Creative budget by spend tier**:
- **$4–30k/month**: founder-led; the production budget is founder *time*. Statics + iPhone founder videos get you to $30k/mo. The founder *should* make the content — creative is the biggest lever, and learning what good looks like is prerequisite to ever KPI-ing and training the team. Work **3 core concepts** only, done well. 60–80% of budget to testing (too small to scale winners hard). Use trial reels to validate.
- **$30–100k/month**: **25% of media budget to production** (at $100k → $25k/mo — more than 99% of brands spend, which is why they stall; it feels too high at first and democratizes with scale). 2–3 UGC creators, 5–6 concepts, ABO + likely a scaling campaign, 30–100 new creatives/month, introduce partnership ads.
- **$100k+/month**: production asymptotes to **~10% of media budget** (at a 25% MER that's ~2.5% of total revenue). Full creative team: creative strategist/scriptwriter, editor, 4–6 creators, designer (part-time/global OK). 8–10 active concepts, 100+ new assets/month, and a financial model tracking cost per asset by source, profit contribution per ad, and ad-level ROI (spend vs production cost).

**The cautionary case study**: $350k/mo brand, 15 live ads, 2 years of rising CAC, over-segmented account (25 campaigns, cost caps — media-buying theatrics compensating for no creative). Fix: relaunch old winners → 60 ads → +$40k spend, CAC −20–30%, +28% new-customer revenue *with zero new production*. Founder still refused production budget; the 60 ads fatigued; the account decayed back. The rejected math: move $10k of the $350k media budget to production → ~40 assets (hook swaps + statics) → ~5% efficiency gain = $17.5k/month saved, or at their 3x, an extra $40–50k/month revenue at flat spend. Reallocating media budget to creative is a *channel-style* reallocation with positive expected value.

**Trial reels hack** (valid as of April 2026): post ad variants as **trial reels** with the CTA cut off, on the page/profile the ad will run through. You get (a) comparative virality data and (b) **retention graphs** that show whether the hook, bridge, or body is failing — before spending a dollar in Ads Manager, even on unpolished V1s. Test ~20 hook variations as trial reels, put only the best ~5 into the account.

### 8. Creative fatigue

Every ad's life is a curve: launch → spend climbs (manually or via Meta) → hits its **maximum daily spend** (e.g., $500–$1,000/day) → holds → decays → off. Area under the curve = **total spend capacity**, which is fixed-ish — but *you shape the curve*: squeeze hard now (high daily spend, fatigues in weeks — right when you must sell through inventory) or run it at $100/day for six months (right when cash/inventory-constrained). Agencies default to squeezing (30–90-day case studies, ROAS KPIs) at the expense of account longevity — misaligned incentives unless they know your inventory/cash position.

**Two causes of fatigue**: (1) the ad is stale — spent its capacity, or too similar to sibling ads (same visuals/concept/audience accelerates it); (2) **the audience is too small** — a hyper-specific persona has a hard daily spend ceiling (sub-4-marathon men can't absorb $10k/day in Australia). B2B illustration: "e-commerce CFOs in Australia doing $100M+" ≈ 1–2k humans — $10k/day means hitting each ~600 times/day. Note that niche audiences *churn* (people finish the marathon and leave; new ones enter) — so hold spend at capacity and rotate creative continuously rather than saturating. Broader callouts raise spend capacity but demand better creative; go specific first, broaden at scale.

**When an ad fatigues** (ranked): (1) iterate — new hooks on the hero asset (the big one); (2) move post ID into a cost-cap campaign (short-term squeeze, accelerates fatigue); (3) move post ID into a scaling campaign (sometimes works); (4) rotate formats (video→image, VSL→UGC, image→long-form-copy static). Duplicating ad sets: explicitly not recommended ("too tacky"). Truth: once fatigued, there's no save — which is *why* you run a portfolio.

### 9. Portfolio & commercial management

Above ~$50k/month, the media buyer's real job is portfolio management on three planes: across **ads** (fight Pareto concentration or accept its risk), across **products** (20% of products take 80% of spend if unmanaged — sell-through skews), and across **commercials** — the P&L vs balance-sheet play. Over-optimizing efficiency/profit can strand cash in unsold inventory ($2M P&L profit + $3M unsold stock = −$1M cash). Sometimes the right move is restructuring the account to push stock at worse ROAS. A media buyer who isn't across inventory is a liability.

### 10. Production: content types, team, briefing, AI

**Three content types by leverage** (recommended effort split):
1. **Winning content replications** — 50–60% of effort. Re-run, re-shoot, hook-swap, and format-translate proven winners; on organic, literally repost the same winners — same performance, new audience each time. Highest-impact activity in paid and organic.
2. **Iterative content** — 20–30%. Fix underperformers (hooks, bridges, formats); tangential moves (working concept → adjacent persona or new offer).
3. **Net-new concepts** — 20–30%. Never 0% (the pipeline dies), but most teams *overweight* net-new. (He does 80–90% net-new in his own organic and the 10–20% replications hold 80% of views — an admitted over-allocation to new.)

**Briefing creators**: output quality = brief quality. When sourcing, demand past examples **plus the brief behind each example** — the one-for-one comparison of what they do with a given input. Default to the most in-depth brief possible; if the creator resists scripts, have them shoot one scripted take and one natural take and run both.

**Team build** (order of hires): video editor (AI-native strongly preferred — unlocks footage that "doesn't exist yet"), statics designer (global/part-time fine), then **creative strategist** — a new role with three sourcing paths: (a) hire copywriters ("creative strategy is primarily copywriting… copywriters are now redundant because LLMs will produce most of it" — they ideate, then drive a brand-built Claude skill that outputs scripts); (b) poach an existing strategist; (c) backfill from a designer/editor (fastest feedback loops: ideate → design → brief). Compensation pattern: base salary + **~3% of ad spend** on the creatives they brief — direct hit-rate incentive; works at mid-size, breaks at large scale (strategists earning $80k/month).

**Where AI belongs** (pipeline: ideation → briefing → production → publishing → analysis): *skip the outsides, maximize the middle.* Don't automate **ideation** — LLMs output the mean; no amount of Reddit-scraping skills differentiates you; a human strategist must validate concepts. **Briefing/scripting** is the sweet spot: a ~20-question Claude skill (persona? concept? offer? length? awareness stage?) that outputs scripts/briefs "99% of the way there." **Production**: heavy leverage — VSLs and statics can be ~90% AI. **Analysis**: dangerous — AI won't respect the metric hierarchy, account-structure context, or commercial objectives, and will confidently mislead core decisions; use it, but human-checked.

**Other production rules**: content-first is the 2026–27 play for smaller brands — daily organic posting trains the creative muscle that compounds into paid (study retention graphs, share/save rates). Creators must be **native to the target region** (Australian accents flop in the US; UK→AU passable; same script, local creator). Read the four books: *Breakthrough Advertising* (Schwartz), *Scientific Advertising* (Hopkins), *Ogilvy on Advertising*, *Influence* (Cialdini).

## Hard rules (numbered)

1. Never turn off an ad inside an ad set that is hitting KPI — Meta is sequencing those creatives on purpose.
2. Judge creative at the ad-set/campaign level on 7-day click (existing customers excluded); never make decisions on ad-level last-click ROAS.
3. Amount spent is the #1 proxy for creative performance; ROAS/CPA second; CPC/CTR and hook/hold rates are diagnostics only.
4. One concept per ad set — persona + angle + offer (+ format) must be coherent within an ad set or targeting and learnings both break.
5. Test in this order: angle → offer → persona → format. Never start with format.
6. Test one variable at a time, 10+ ads per concept; at a ~5% hit rate expect ~20 ads per winner.
7. Never add new ads to a winning ad set (it resets learning); launch a new ad set for the same concept.
8. Never kill a winner to "move it to a scaling campaign" — raise its budget; optionally duplicate the post ID.
9. Cold frequency above 2 = act: add concept diversity and volume immediately.
10. Personas must be hyper-specific problem archetypes, not demographics; broad personas produce forgettable creative.
11. Agitate problems, don't call out personas — persona callouts get skipped and steer Andromeda wrong if mismatched.
12. Introduce the product as late in the asset as possible; blend the bridge — no step-change from education to pitch.
13. 70–90% of creative production volume goes to top-of-funnel awareness stages.
14. Distrust DPA ROAS: exclude existing customers, check incremental attribution, and never scale DPAs as a cold strategy.
15. Every ad will fatigue and has a finite spend capacity — run a portfolio; never let 1–3 ads carry the account.
16. Spend on production: founder time to $30k/mo, 25% of media budget from $30–100k/mo, ~10% above $100k/mo — creative is a revenue driver, not a cost center.
17. Volume above the 7/10 quality bar beats winner-picking; nobody can pick winners among good ads.
18. Effort split: 50–60% replicating winners, 20–30% iterating, 20–30% net-new — never 0% net-new.
19. Use AI for briefing/scripting and production, not for ideation or unsupervised analysis.
20. Use native-region creators; same script, local accent.

## Exact language worth keeping

1. *"The creative that you load in is the targeting."* — the single sentence that explains post-Andromeda Meta.
2. *"After two impressions of an identical creative, probability of purchase declines precipitously."* — the reason volume and rotation are non-negotiable.
3. *"Cold ads almost never have a frequency above a two. And if they do, you have a creative fatigue issue."* — the fastest account health check.
4. *"Do not turn a creative off when you are hitting KPI."* — protects sequencing from last-click misreads.
5. *"Amount spent becomes the best proxy for performance when looking at creative."* — counterintuitive, decision-changing.
6. *"The hook of an ad is a play towards relevancy"* — kills clickbait thinking; hooks promise relevance, not tricks.
7. *"Persona callouts are super overrated… you'll always see much better performance agitating on a problem."* — directly actionable for every script.
8. *"You are better off making five ads for [the specific persona] than five for [the broad one]."* — specificity beats TAM at sub-scale.
9. *"UGC as a concept doesn't work. It is the script, it is the persona, it is the angle, it is the offer that is contained within this type of content that is what works."* — format is a container, not a strategy.
10. *"You actually want to introduce your product as late as possible in the creative."* — inverts retention-curve intuition; fewer, hotter viewers convert more in total.
11. *"The complexity of the ad account should be a product of the complexity of the business."* — the anti-template principle for structure.
12. *"Every single creative will fatigue. Every single creative has a maximum spend threshold."* — the premise of portfolio management.
13. *"If it's a good creative, no one will be able to guess which one will win, so put them all into the account."* — quality bar + volume, not winner-picking.
14. *"The belief that image ads are bottom of funnel and video ads are top of funnel is fundamentally a skill issue."* — permission to build TOF statics.
15. *"LLMs just output the mean."* — why ideation stays human and briefing/production get automated.

## How to apply this to any account

A repeatable procedure for mapping the system onto any brand's Meta account:

1. **Identify your spend tier — it sets every downstream parameter** (§7). Founder-led (up to ~$30k/mo): production budget = founder time, statics + iPhone founder videos, **3 core concepts**, 60–80% of budget effectively testing. Mid ($30–100k/mo): **25% of media budget to production**, 5–6 concepts, 2–3 UGC creators, 30–100 new creatives/month, introduce partnership ads. Scaled ($100k+/mo): **~10% of media budget to production**, 8–10 concepts, 100+ assets/month, full creative team with a per-asset financial model. Do not run a bigger tier's concept count or team on a smaller tier's spend — "the complexity of the ad account should be a product of the complexity of the business."

2. **Translate "ROAS" into your business's true KPI.** E-commerce: 7-day-click ROAS with existing customers excluded, at ad-set/campaign level. Lead-gen / high-ticket / applications: cost per qualified lead, application, or booked call on 7-day click, judged at the same levels. Whatever the KPI, the metric hierarchy holds unchanged: amount spent first, KPI second, CPC/CTR at the fringes, hook/hold rates as diagnostics only. Expect the "closer" asset in a sequence (often a VSL or heavy proof piece) to show great last-click numbers while the top-of-funnel openers look bad — that is the system working, not a pruning opportunity.

3. **Build the persona bank before writing anything** (§3). Manually draft 30–40 hyper-specific problem archetypes (problems, desires, buying triggers, language, required proof — never demographics), then use an LLM only to *expand* the hand-built list with deep business context attached. Keep the best handful per tier (3 concepts founder-led, 5–6 mid, 8–10 scaled).

4. **Structure every concept as persona + angle + offer (+ format).** Audit what's currently live: assign each running ad to a concept; if the count of *distinct* concepts is 1–3, that's the bottleneck regardless of ad count. New concepts change at least one variable, tested in the priority order angle → offer → persona → format.

5. **Set up the account skeleton**: one testing campaign (ABO by default; CBO only for a temporary efficiency squeeze), **ad sets = concepts**, minimum 3 ads per ad set and 10+ per concept test; a retargeting campaign on existing customers so the cold campaigns can exclude them; new shoots for a winning concept go into a *new* ad set, never into the winner.

6. **Set the volume commitment**: floor of 1 new ad per $1,000 in monthly spend; better, back-propagate from the new-customer revenue target (mean spend per ad × average KPI-basis ROAS = expected revenue per ad; target ÷ that = ads/month). Allocate production volume 70–90% TOF / ~20% MOF / 5–10% BOF, with BOF mapped to the objection mix (price/trust/quality/timing).

7. **Benchmarks to watch**: cold frequency ≤2 (weekly audit; a breach triggers a hook-swap batch, not a budget change); amount spent as the within-ad-set ranking; KPI at ad-set level on 7-day click; a CPC/CTR early-kill fringe calibrated from the account's own history; hook rate/hold rate only to decide *what* to fix (hook vs bridge vs body). Define kill/scale criteria before launch, not after.

8. **Iteration cadence**: shoot 6+ hook takes per body in every batch and bank them; run the effort split 50–60% replicating winners / 20–30% iterating / 20–30% net-new; pre-filter hooks free with trial reels (~20 variants posted CTA-less on the run-through profile, best ~5 into the account); at a ~5% hit rate, a first batch of 10–12 ads producing zero winners is a normal outcome, not a verdict on the concepts — iterate hooks and bridges off the diagnostics before declaring an angle dead.

9. **Small-TAM adjustment** (niche B2B, professional verticals, high-ticket): the audience has a hard daily spend ceiling and frequency climbs fast if creative is samey. Shape the fatigue curve for longevity (modest daily spend, continuous creative rotation as the audience churns) rather than squeezing; make the frequency check the number-one weekly health metric; diversify hooks aggressively; run ads through a person's profile (founder, practitioner, creator) rather than the brand page for organic feel, and graduate to partnership ads through customers'/creators' handles as proof accumulates.

**Worked micro-examples at three tiers** (all parameters from the source):

- **Founder-led, ~$3–5k/month** (single-product startup or high-ticket service): the founder shoots iPhone selfie/POV videos and statics; 3 concepts = 3 distinct angles on the sharpest problem archetype, each in its own ABO ad set with 3–4 ads. KPI = cost per purchase or per qualified application on 7-day click at ad-set level. Pre-filter every hook via trial reels on the founder's profile; run the ads through the founder's personal handle. Expect the whole first batch to spend before any winner appears (hit-rate math), and budget the founder's time for hook-swap batches as the first iteration wave.
- **Mid, ~$30k/month DTC brand**: $7.5k/month to production (25%); 5–6 concepts across 2–3 personas; ~30 new ads/month (1 per $1k); 2–3 UGC creators plus a statics program run on the cost-vs-contribution-margin math; ABO testing campaign + optional post-ID scaling campaign; introduce partnership ads through the creators' handles; rebalance the creative mix toward 60/40–70/30 pain:desire; weekly frequency audit and a CPC fringe threshold from account history.
- **Scaled, ~$300k/month**: ~$30k/month production budget (~10% of media); 8–10 active concepts; back-propagated volume (e.g., mean spend per ad $1,000 × 3x = $3,000 expected revenue per ad → a $300k new-customer target needs ~100 creatives/month); full team (strategist on base + ~3% of spend on their creatives, AI-native editor, 4–6 creators, designer) with a financial model tracking cost and ROI per asset; the job shifts to portfolio management across ads, products, and commercials — and the $350k/month cautionary case is the exact failure mode: never let the live-ad count sit at 15 while media-buying theatrics (over-segmentation, cost caps) compensate for missing creative.
