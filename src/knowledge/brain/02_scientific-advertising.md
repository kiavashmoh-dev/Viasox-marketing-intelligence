# Scientific Advertising — Claude Hopkins (study distillation)
*Part of The Marketing Brain (Portable) — a universal knowledge base. Full book studied; all 21 chapters preserved.*

**Source type:** Foundational direct-response text (1923). Every claim derived from keyed (tracked) ads, coupon returns, and cost-per-customer data — not opinion.

**When to consult — THE authority on:** testing discipline (keyed ads, test campaigns, small spends deciding big spends); specificity of claims (exact numbers vs. platitudes); offers (samples, free trials, risk-reversal, entry-offer structure); headlines-as-salesmanship (the headline *selects* the prospect); cost-per-result thinking; and what NOT to spend on (market education, habit change, prevention appeals, decoration, dealer help). NOT the authority on awareness stages (Schwartz), copy mechanics (Bly), or brand architecture (Neumeier). When a decision could be settled by "run it and read the numbers," Hopkins is the tiebreaker.

## Core thesis (one paragraph)

Advertising is salesmanship multiplied — nothing more — and because it talks to thousands at enormous cost instead of one person for free, it must be held to *stricter* standards than a salesman, never looser. Its only purpose is to make sales, measured by cost per customer or cost per dollar of sale. Since human judgment errs "often nine times in ten," no question of headline, claim, offer, picture, or plan is ever settled by argument, taste, or authority — only by keyed returns from real buyers, "the court of last resort." Test small, trace everything: when a thousand customers tell you what they cost and what they buy, you know almost exactly what a million will cost and buy. Done this way advertising ceases to be a gamble and becomes "one of the safest business ventures" — and every departure from tested salesmanship (fine writing, big type, cleverness, boasting, brevity for its own sake) is a known, measurable tax on results.

## The complete principles — chapter by chapter

**1. How Advertising Laws Are Established.** Advertising became a science because agencies ran thousands of keyed campaigns and recorded every result. Instruments: keyed ads, traced returns, coupons; where direct tracing is impossible, compare town against town by cost of sales. Reply counts aren't final — one ad brings worthless replies, another valuable ones — so conclusions rest only on cost per customer or cost per dollar of sale. Mail order, traced "to the fraction of a penny," established the basic laws. The remaining uncertainties concern people and products, never methods: "We cannot say that an article will be popular, but we know how to find out very quickly." Individuality in campaigns is essential, imitation a reproach — but variations sit atop immutable fundamentals.

**2. Just Salesmanship.** Advertising is salesmanship in print; every question answers to: "Would this help a salesman sell the goods? Would it help me sell them if I met the buyer in person?" It is NOT for general effect or name-recognition. At $10 per word, every ad must be a super-salesman, and a mediocre ad damages *all* your trade. Fine writing is a disadvantage — it takes attention from the subject and "reveals the hook"; any apparent effort to sell creates resistance. The test kills slogans, clever conceits, big type (people read all they care to in 8-point; bigger is loud conversation at doubled cost), and arbitrary brevity — no salesman with a live prospect is confined to a word count. Ads aren't entertainment; writers who seek applause have abandoned their part. Write to one typical buyer, never "people in the mass." The best ad men sell door-to-door or interview hundreds first — "guesswork is very expensive" — and the maker's own knowledge misleads him: his interests are not the customer's. The two great failures: selling what people don't want, and ads written to please the seller.

**3. Offer Service.** People are selfish; they care nothing for your profit and seek service for themselves. "Buy my brand" is not a popular appeal — "the best ads ask no one to buy." Base ads entirely on service: wanted information, user advantages, a sample, buying the first package, sending on approval so the customer proves the claims without risk. Proofs: the brush canvasser opening "I was sent here to *give* you a brush" (she picks one, wants others, reciprocates with an order); the coffee wagon leaving a half-pound to try — "always some service"; the sewing-machine motor sent for a free week with a demonstrator — "resistless," nine in ten trials became sales. "People can be coaxed but not driven. Whatever they do they do to please themselves."

**4. Mail Order Advertising — What It Teaches.** The school every ad man must graduate from: cost and result are nakedly visible, "false theories melt away like snowflakes in the sun," and one learns his judgment errs nine times in ten. Three ads for the same $5 article pulled replies at 85¢, $14.20, and 41¢ — untraced advertisers pay 2–35× what sales need cost. Proven mail order copy teaches: small type (larger = paying double for the same sales), no wasted space, no palaver, always a coupon (readers forget a buying decision within five minutes — the coupon is the cut-out reminder), pictures only when they earn their space (the incubator ad's decorative chicken silhouette: +50% space, +50% cost per reply, zero added sales), and complete stories — "The more you tell the more you sell," never disproved in any test. Bigger ads bring proportionally bigger returns *only* if the space is as well used. Mead Cycle wouldn't change a word for $10,000 — the residue of years of comparisons. Every departure from these principles is a priced frivolity: "let us know the cost of our pride."

**5. Headlines.** An ad can be ignored, so the headline's whole purpose is to pick out the people you can interest — "Hey there, Bill Jones" in a crowd. Clever or blind headlines attract many more of the *wrong* people while your prospects never realize the ad is for them (the most-beautiful-woman story, unread under "Egyptian Psychology"). People do read ads — 20% of a paper's readers have cut a single coupon — but they decide at a glance by headline and picture. Hopkins spent hours per headline, discarding scores, and held keyed returns on nearly 2,000 headlines for one product: a headline change alone commonly multiplies returns 5–10×. "The appeals we like best will rarely prove best, because we do not know enough people to average up their desires" — learn the winning appeal class by experiment, weight headline usage by tested proportion, run many ads for overlapping audiences. "The Sportiest of Sport Bodies" might outpull a universal-joint headline fifty to one.

**6. Psychology.** Human nature is perpetual — "the same today as in the time of Caesar" — so these lessons never need unlearning. **Curiosity** is among the strongest incentives (Puffed Wheat failed until "Foods shot from guns"). **Cheapness is not an appeal** — people want bargains, not cheapness. **People judge by price** (the $750,000 painting ignored until its price is read; "we paid $100,000 for this formula"). **Proximity of guarantee**: a dealer's signed warrant (a neighbor, not a stranger) made a fortune. **Pay-after beats money-back**: "pay in a week if you like them" proved many times as impressive (the two-horses parable), and people prove honest. **Ownership**: the buyer's name in gilt sold hundreds of thousands of book sets; people claim what already "belongs" to them. **Class-limited offers** (veterans, executives) far outpull general offers. **Confidence courts comparison**: "Try our rivals too" cured substitution where "beware of substitutes" failed. **Free cheapens, paid trial dignifies**: the maker who *paid retail* for the customer's first package succeeded; the giver of free packages failed — "it is hard to pay for an article which has once been free"; a sample *asked for after reading your story* meets a primed mind. **Created persona**: the installment-clothing house fronted by a motherly woman who signed every ad drove its established rival out of business. An identical offer made a different way brings multiplied returns.

**7. Being Specific.** "Platitudes and generalities roll off the human understanding like water from a duck." Superlatives are worse than nothing — they suggest looseness with truth and discount everything else you say. But "a man who makes a specific claim is either telling the truth or a lie," and people don't expect advertisers to lie — so definite statements are accepted at full weight, and an argument's weight is *multiplied* by making it specific: "three and one-third times the light" (implies tests); "Our net profit is 3 percent" broke a commoditized "lowest prices" war; "Our profit is 9 percent" plus $735 of itemized hidden costs. The shaving cream won a saturated field with "Multiplies itself in lather 250 times," "Softens the beard in one minute," "the final result of testing and comparing 130 formulas." The "78-second shave" beat every "quick shave." The brewer who described plate-glass cooling rooms, bottles washed four times, water from 4,000 feet, 1,018 yeast experiments — facts true of *every* brewer — told them first while rivals cried "Pure," and made beer advertising's greatest success. "If a claim is worth making, make it in the most impressive way."

**8. Tell Your Full Story.** Readers give you one reading, decide for or against, and that decision operates against a second reading — so "when you once get a person's attention, then is the time to accomplish all you can ever hope with him." Present every tested claim in every ad; omit one and you lose the percentage it alone converts. Serial ads are the greatest folly — the installments never connect. Perfected ads run unchanged for years because the audience is always new readers: "In every ad consider only new customers" — present users don't read your ads at all. You are the salesman finally admitted to the busy man's office; this is your one chance. No fixed rule on brevity — a sentence can sell chewing gum — but the story must be complete: the Marmon sold a Rolls-Royce-inclined buyer by offering columns of information while the famous marques offered dignified silence; a car buyer "will read a volume about it if the volume is interesting." Brief ads are never keyed: "Take the opinion of nobody... who knows nothing about his returns."

**9. Art in Advertising.** Pictures are expensive in space — often a third to half the campaign — so use one only when it sells better than the same space set in type; never to decorate or amuse. Mail order advertisers have pictures down to a science; none uses expensive artwork. Where pictures earn their space: Arrow Collar men whom others envy in surroundings others covet; correspondence-school men taking upward steps; beauty ads showing admired women *plus a fascinated man* (show the beauty in use). Never eccentric or frivolous: "people do not patronize a clown"; men never joke about business or home; an odd picture overshadows the headline, your main appeal. Minor questions (art quality, repetition, color) settle per line by test: repetition is probably an economy (readers are always new); color pays only where it approximates exhibition (food). Minor economies beside the basic appeal, which halves or multiplies everything.

**10. Things Too Costly.** Weigh every project on a known scale of cost and result. **Changing habits** (shaving soap to bearded Russian peasants) is prohibitive. **Educating a market** costs more than it returns and rivals share the converts: toothbrush-habit converts tested at $20–25 each — "what cannot be done on a large scale profitably cannot be done on a small scale," so devote not one line to it. Market creation works only through wide co-operation (combined orange growers); the shrewd play is to watch a trend create a desire, then arrive to satisfy it (yeasts, antiseptics). **Low consumption caps advertising**: a $1.50 germicide customer whose bottle lasts years may not repay in a decade; single-sale mail order customers rarely cost under $2.50. **Prevention doesn't sell**: "People will do much to cure trouble, but people in general will do little to prevent it" — the leading toothpaste never features tooth troubles (tests proved them unappealing); a 1-in-100 eczema claim can *destroy* the near-universal complexion claim beside it. **Narrow appeals multiply waste**: an asthma remedy reaching one reader in a hundred pays ~20× the cost per result of a one-in-five appeal. Minor proven claims: include in every ad, feature in a tested *percentage* of headlines — never guessed.

**11. Information.** Gain full information before writing — weeks of reading if needed — because "some one fact may be the keystone of success." The caffeine-free coffee campaign hinged on one article in a thousand read: caffeine's stimulation arrives two hours after drinking, so removing it removes nothing people feel. "Genius is the art of taking pains." The apparatus: 130 men interviewing consumers for weeks; letters to 12,000 physicians; questionnaires to tens of thousands. The pork-and-beans canvass found only 4% used *any* canned brand — so the winning appeal fought home baking, not rival brands. Collect every rival's literature. Learn the user's annual spend (what a customer is worth), total consumption, and the percentage of readers your appeal reaches (waste circulation sets cost). Verify claims with chemists — makers are remarkably often wrong, and an untrue claim is a boomerang — then exactify: "large food value" became "425 calories per pint — equal to six eggs." The ad must look simple; behind it lie reams of data. "This is no lazy man's field."

**12. Strategy.** Advertising is "much like war, minus the venom": you're capturing others' trade, so you need intelligence, ammunition, and force-multiplying strategy. **Name**: the right name is an ad in itself (Cream of Wheat); coined names (Kodak) are exclusive but must be given meaning; shareable names (Toasted Corn Flakes) hand your demand to rivals. **Price**: high price creates resistance; the greatest profits usually come from volume at small margin (Ford, Campbell's) — but low-consumption lines need high margins, and in lines judged by price, higher price can itself persuade. **Competition**: habit-owning lines are near-impregnable, invaded only through a convincing advantage or superior salesmanship-in-print. Win the individual before the crowd — picture one man on a Pullman using his favorite soap; what would you say *to him*? "We cannot go after thousands of men until we learn how to win one." Even a me-too maker must find the impressive thing others haven't told — "people don't quit habits without reason." Build anti-substitution defenses at the start (Vaseline monopolized its demand; Royal Baking Powder and Horlick's left the door open forever). Many problems lie in the foundations.

**13. Use of Samples.** The product plus the mental atmosphere around it is its own best salesman, so samples — however expensive — usually form the *cheapest* selling method. They: (1) let you say "Free," which alone multiplies readership enough to pay for them; (2) convert interest into action via the coupon before the five-minute forgetting window; (3) capture the name of a half-sold prospect for follow-up — that reader may not see your ad again for six months; (4) key your ads; (5) refer customers to stocked dealers. **Never charge for samples**: asking a dime adds 40¢–$1 to reply cost, kills "Free," and cost-sharing fails because *before conversion, half price is nearly as hard to get as full price* — you are the courting party. **Make redemption effortless and local**: mailed inquiries ran 70¢ vs. 18–22¢ store-presented; offered three channels, 70% chose the telephone. Control abuse cheaply ("only one sample to a home") — checking repeaters cost more than the repeaters did. **Never sample promiscuously**: doorstep handouts and store demos cheapen the product and meet no prepared mind. "Give samples to interested people only... to people to whom you have told your story. First create an atmosphere of respect, a desire, an expectation" — the sample then confirms the claims. Judge by cost per customer.

**14. Getting Distribution.** Never start advertising without distribution — a venture can't profit if nine in ten converts can't find the goods. Start local, town by town, then go national. Tactics: name stocked dealers in early ads (the rest rush to be included); let sample coupons name stores, centering demand that forces supply; supply samples only with a purchase requirement; and — most powerful — the store-redeemable full-package coupon with ad proofs mailed to dealers in advance: every coupon is a cash sale at full profit no dealer will surrender — this attained national distribution *in one week* (Palmolive, Puffed Grains). Don't mail samples to out-of-town inquirers — place them with a local store that can then supply demand. Gifting dealers stock is costly; consignment breeds resentment. Lost time lets rivals ahead.

**15. Test Campaigns.** "Almost any question can be answered, cheaply, quickly and finally, by a test campaign... not by arguments around a table. Go to the court of last resort — the buyers of your product." Projects you'd laugh at succeed; sure things fall down. Run the real offer in four or five towns; learn cost per customer started, repeat rate, and how long till profit returns the selling cost. "Now we let the thousands decide what the millions will do... We establish averages on a small scale, and those averages always hold." A test costs $3,000–5,000 and even failures return most of it in sales; sometimes ad cost returns before the bills are due — advertising without investment. The asymmetry: "playing on the safe side of a hundred to one shot." The world's largest advertiser built twenty-six winners this way. Tests answer product questions too: a ~$1,000 coupon-plus-letters test killed a new product form the whole company was sure of; a doubted second form won 91% approval. Testing never stops: fifty plans over five years cut one advertiser's selling cost 75% — four times the previous best. Tests even settle vendor claims: let the new agency prove itself in a few towns. Never state a supposition before proving it.

**16. Leaning On Dealers.** Expect no active dealer help: they're busy, thin-margined on advertised goods, and push their own brands — as you would. Discounts and loading deals (one case free in ten) rarely raise total sales; they swing trade between stores, and free goods mean advertising must sell 10% more for the same return. Advertising-made converts stick; casual-recommendation buyers follow the next recommendation. Window displays may hand one dealer the local trade without adding one total sale — test town against town; many experienced advertisers spend nothing on displays. Your object is to buy new customers at a price that pays: if a customer costs a dollar, every wasted dollar forfeits one. Do your own selling; let dealers fill the orders you bring.

**17. Individuality.** Stand out in a *pleasing* way — eccentricity repels, but "doing admirable things in a different way gives one a great advantage." Give the advertiser a becoming style suited to his audience: rugged honesty, good fellow, or authority as the line demands. Signed ads give personal authority — a proud man talking, not a "soulless corporation"; making a man famous makes his product famous. Once a personality wins, never change it: new writers must play the part as an actor plays it. "Appearing different every time we meet never builds up confidence"; appeals must seem to come from the heart, the same heart always. The right individuality compounds into ever-increasing prestige.

**18. Negative Advertising.** Never attack a rival — the selfish purpose shows, and the best media won't carry it. Never sell from the dark side: "Don't show the wrinkles you propose to remove, but the face as it will appear. Your customers know all about wrinkles." Pretty teeth, not bad teeth; successful men, not failures; "picture what others wish to be, not what they may be now"; "picture envied people, not the envious." Tell people what to do, not what to avoid; assume compliance ("Send now for this sample"), never suggest neglect. Tested figure: **the positive ad outpulls the negative four to one.** "Before and after" ads are follies of the past.

**19. Letter Writing.** All ad principles govern letters. The $50-million-a-year buyer trashes a score of letters a minute; the kept ones offered, at a glance, something he wanted. The magazine mailing five million letters first tests ~25 versions on a thousand prospects each and mails only the proven winner. A letter to an inquirer is a salesman visiting an interested prospect: you *know* what created the interest — follow up on that exact line, complete the impression already made, never open a new argument on a guess. Fine stationery *lessens* effect — it signals selling on other lines than merit. Above all, force immediate action — people are dilatory and postponed action is forgotten: deadlines, expiring premiums, gifts for promptness (the gift-with-order card beat rival catalogs; a one-week premium moved hundreds of thousands of six-package purchases). "Strike while the iron is hot."

**20. A Name That Helps.** The name occupies your most prominent display, so it should tell a selling story: Cream of Wheat, Dutch Cleanser, Minute Tapioca, Holeproof — "almost complete advertisements in themselves," protectable yet descriptive. Meaningless coined names (Kodak, Lux) can be given meaning by long advertising, but most never attain it — until then the name deserves no display: the service, not the name, is what matters. Ingredient names (Palmolive) invite substitution; descriptive common names (Toasted Corn Flakes, Malted Milk) let rivals harvest your demand — "some have lost for their originators four-fifths of the trade they developed." A patent-tied name expires with the patent (Aspirin, Castoria). Frivolous names prohibit respect. When a common name is unavoidable, the best auxiliary is a **man's name** — it shows a man proud of his creation.

**21. Good Business.** The millstream parable: the same water that idly turned a wooden wheel, given a turbine, ran a manufacturing plant — identical power, scientific application. Everywhere advertising power turns mill-wheels: men spending five dollars for a one-dollar job, getting 30% back when they might get 150%, running unkeyed ads to satisfy a whim. A $700,000-a-year advertiser didn't know if his advertising was worth anything; Hopkins' one-week proof: "End an ad with an offer to pay five dollars to anyone who writes you that he read the ad through. The scarcity of replies will amaze you." The crowded "ugly" ads you dislike are the keyed ones that pay. "Bunk has lost its power. Sophistry is being displaced by actuality" — men and methods will be measured by known returns, and those who can meet the test welcome it.

## Hard rules (numbered)

1. Judge every ad by the salesman test: "Would this help a salesman sell the goods face-to-face?" If not, cut it.
2. The only purpose of advertising is sales — measured by cost per customer or cost per dollar of sale, never replies, attention, or "general effect."
3. Key (track) every ad. Take the verdict of nobody who doesn't know his returns; untested judgment errs nine times in ten.
4. The headline *selects* the prospect. Blind/clever headlines attract the wrong crowd and hide the ad from your buyer.
5. Test headlines relentlessly: a headline change alone commonly multiplies returns 5–10×; the appeals you like best rarely prove best.
6. Never ask people to buy; offer service. People can be coaxed but not driven.
7. Be specific: generalities are discounted to zero and taint your real claims; exact figures are accepted at full weight.
8. Tell the full story in every ad — every tested claim, every time. Never serialize; you get one reading.
9. Write to one typical individual, never "the market" — you can't win thousands until you can win one.
10. Consider only new customers in every ad — current customers don't read your ads.
11. Fine writing, cleverness, entertainment, boasting, and frivolity are disadvantages: they reveal the hook and create resistance.
12. No arbitrary brevity: "The more you tell the more you sell" — never disproved in any test.
13. Bigger space pays only in proportion to use: double space with double-size type = paying double for the same sales.
14. Pictures must be salesmen that earn their space or be cut; never decorative, never eccentric (it overshadows the headline).
15. Positive outpulls negative 4:1. Show the attained state and envied people; tell people what to do; assume compliance in CTAs.
16. Never attack competitors; courting comparison ("Try our rivals too") beats warnings.
17. Never spend to educate a market or change habits alone — converts cost multiples of their value and rivals share them. Ride desires already created.
18. Sell the cure, not the prevention; a narrow claim can destroy a broad one in the same ad.
19. Never charge for a sample or split its cost — before conversion, half price is nearly as hard to get as full price. "Free" pays for itself.
20. But never cheapen with promiscuous free: paying retail for the customer's first package respects the product; giveaways cheapen it.
21. Sample only interested people who've read your story and acted to ask — create respect and expectation first.
22. Make response effortless and local (18–22¢ store-presented vs. 70¢ mailed; 70% chose telephone).
23. Demand immediate action: coupon, deadline, expiring premium. A decision postponed is forgotten in five minutes.
24. Limit offers to a defined class — entitlement outpulls general offers.
25. Attach ownership and a person: the prospect's name on the thing; a real named human signing the ads — then never change that persona once it wins.
26. Research before writing: canvasses, rival literature, verified figures. One found fact can be the campaign's keystone; unverified claims are boomerangs.
27. Know the arithmetic before launching: annual spend per user, repeat rate, % of audience reached. Narrow appeals (1-in-100) multiply cost per result ~20×.
28. Test small before spending big: real offer, few markets, traced results. "Averages established on a small scale always hold." Expand on proven numbers, never argument.
29. Never stop testing after success — plan after plan against the control (fifty plans in five years cut selling cost 75%).
30. Don't start advertising without distribution; pull distribution with demand, don't buy it with gifts or consignment.
31. Don't lean on dealers, displays, or trade deals — they shift trade without adding converts. Buy your own customers.
32. Choose a name that sells and is protectable; a shared, frivolous, or patent-bound name is a permanent tax. Fallback: a man's name.
33. Price strategically: volume-at-small-profit usually wins, but people judge by price, and in some lines higher price itself persuades.

## Exact language worth keeping (verbatim)

1. **"Advertising is salesmanship. Its principles are the principles of salesmanship."** — The axiom; ends every ads-as-content debate.
2. **"The only purpose of advertising is to make sales. It is profitable or unprofitable according to its actual sales."** — The metric mandate.
3. **"Don't think of people in the mass. That gives you a blurred view. Think of a typical individual... likely to want what you sell."** — The one-reader rule; cure for committee-copy.
4. **"The best ads ask no one to buy."** — Service-first offer architecture in seven words.
5. **"People can be coaxed but not driven. Whatever they do they do to please themselves."** — Why hard CTAs and guilt fail; why offers work.
6. **"False theories melt away like snowflakes in the sun."** — What tracked spend does to opinion; the case for keying everything.
7. **"The more you tell the more you sell."** — The long-copy law, "never failed to prove out so in any test we know."
8. **"The purpose of a headline is to pick out people you can interest."** — Headline = audience filter, not attention stunt.
9. **"It is not uncommon for a change in headlines to multiply returns from five to ten times over."** — Advertising's highest-leverage test, with its multiplier.
10. **"Platitudes and generalities roll off the human understanding like water from a duck."** — The specificity mandate.
11. **"A man who makes a specific claim is either telling the truth or a lie."** — Why exact numbers get believed and superlatives don't.
12. **"In every ad consider only new customers. People using your product are not going to read your ads."** — Kills insider copy.
13. **"People will do much to cure trouble, but people in general will do little to prevent it."** — Angle-selection law: present pain over future risk.
14. **"We cannot go after thousands of men until we learn how to win one."** — Strategy begins with one named prospect.
15. **"Now we let the thousands decide what the millions will do."** — The test-campaign creed; "averages always hold."
16. **"Don't show the wrinkles you propose to remove, but the face as it will appear. Your customers know all about wrinkles."** — Show the outcome, not the lived problem. (Companion: "the positive ad outpulls the other four to one.")
17. **"Genius is the art of taking pains."** — The research ethic: the winning fact is buried in the thousandth article.

## How to apply this to any brand

**1. Make specificity the claims policy.** Convert every general claim into a checkable number or fact: not "save money" but the exact dollar contrast; not "proven program" but "N clients through it in M months"; not "high quality" but the process facts behind it. Then run the Schlitz play: narrate the *ordinary mechanics* of how your product/service actually works — facts your competitors could also claim but haven't told first. The first teller owns them. Treat every superlative ("best," "transform," "world-class") as worse than blank space — it discounts the true claims beside it.

**2. Design the lead magnet / trial / demo by the sampling laws.** (a) Zero friction and zero charge at the entry — any micro-payment or gate multiplies cost per lead. (b) Sample only *after the story*: the ad must build the claim and expectation before the click, so the prospect arrives primed; a cold giveaway is a doorstep handout. (c) Capture the half-sold prospect's contact — they may not see your ad again for months; follow up on the *same* line that created the interest, never a new argument. (d) Free must not read cheap: position the free asset as something you invested in heavily. (e) Class-limit the offer ("for [licensed professionals / founders / practice owners] only") — tested entitlement outpulls general offers. (f) The application/booking step is the coupon: capture the decision inside the five-minute window.

**3. Run every paid launch as a Hopkins test campaign.** A small daily budget IS the four-or-five-town test. Key everything: each ad → tracked path → cost per *final conversion event* (purchase, qualified application, booked call) — impressions, CTR, and raw lead counts are "worthless replies." Test headlines/hooks first (the 5–10× lever). Expect your favorite appeal to lose. Once ~30–50 conversions establish the averages, they hold at scale — expand on numbers, never enthusiasm. Pre-compute the arithmetic before spending: allowable cost per conversion vs. price and margin, and how long cash takes to return. Then never stop testing plans against the control.

**4. Sell positive, sell the cure.** Build creative on the attained state — the envied customer, not the suffering one ("your customers know all about wrinkles"). Pain may appear in the hook as a *selector*, but body and imagery point at the bright after-state; CTAs assume compliance ("Send now…"), never scold. Prevention-shaped angles ("avoid future problems") underperform cure-shaped ones ("fix the pain you have now") — wrap prevention offers in a present-tense gain. And never attack competitors: confidence courts comparison.

**5. Divide the labor: ads select, the long asset tells the full story, one persona fronts it all.** The ad's only job is to hail the right prospect out of the feed ("Hey there, Bill Jones"); the sales page/VSL/consult is where "the more you tell the more you sell" lives — every tested claim, every objection, in one complete telling, because the prospect gives you one reading. Never serialize the argument across retargeting ads. Write every ad for the brand-new prospect. And if a real, named founder/expert fronts the brand (Hopkins' created-persona case: identical offers, multiplied returns), keep that persona absolutely consistent — new writers play the part as an actor plays it.

**Worked micro-examples:**
- **DTC supplement:** replace "premium ingredients" with the sourcing/dosage numbers + the manufacturing narration nobody in the niche has told; free sample only behind a full-story advertorial; judge ads on cost per first purchase and repeat rate.
- **SaaS:** the free trial is the sample — no credit card at the gate (rule 19), but only offered after the story (feature-page or demo-video first); onboarding emails follow up on the exact feature that drove the signup.
- **High-ticket service ($5k+):** class-limit the application, keep the consult free but positioned as selective; pre-compute allowable CAC from price × close rate before day one of spend.
