## The complete advice

### Section 1 — How to think

1. **Think like a mad scientist, not a marketer.** "If you start to think like a marketer, you've actually already lost." Marketer-thinking is rigid; scientist-thinking picks apart every variable and tests everything. He credits this frame for being able to deconstruct *why* things work (e.g., Sam Sulek's success) rather than accepting surface explanations.

2. **Everything in marketing boils down to one word: contrast.** The best marketers create the widest range of contrast — the ability to stand out from the background, like black text on white. Everyone is bombarded with marketing daily, so the job is not "good marketing" but marketing that stands out from everything else. Every ad, email, and content piece must create maximum contrast in the customer's mind.

3. **The entry question for any market: "How can I do the exact opposite?"** When entering a new market, he studies top competitors and top creators, then asks how to do the polar opposite of what everyone is being bombarded with. "The question you need to answer is not how do I market better? It's literally how do I look nothing like anybody else?"

4. **Your job is to break the habit.** Humans are habitual creatures. Running ads = breaking the scrolling habit. Sending email = breaking the inbox-purging habit (99% of subject lines look identical and get deleted). "Your goal is to break the pattern."

5. **Assume you're in stage 4+ market sophistication.** Citing Schwartz's stages via the cigarette industry: stage 1 = a billboard that just says "cigarettes"; stage 2 = competing on claims; stage 3 = claims become unbelievable, so you build a mechanism; up through stage 5. For "the far, far majority of markets, with very few exceptions, you are in stage four sophistication or greater" — so you cannot come in average.

6. **Aim for monopoly of a small market (Zero to One / Thiel).** Don't try to capture a slice of a mega-market; capture 100% of a smaller one. You can't own 100% of a market that has competitors everywhere — which is why contrast plus segment-carving matters.

7. **Mass desire cannot be created — only transferred (Schwartz).** "If you're trying to educate your market, just give up. Instead, you should channel the existing desire into your product." One of his "most fundamental rules of marketing of all time."

8. **Sam Sulek case study: contrast, not authenticity.** Fitness YouTube = overproduced thumbnails, influencer voice, 9–15 minute videos. Sulek (whom Mark found at ~10,000 subscribers): no thumbnails (raw video frames), 30+ minute videos, flat delivery — the exact opposite on every variable. Mark believes he's one of the fastest-growing fitness accounts of all time. Most people credit "authenticity"; the real driver is a high degree of contrast across many variables (authenticity included as one of them).

9. **Purple Ocean Theory: enter proven demand, carve a hyper-specific segment, own 100% of it.** Blue ocean (zero competition) = zero proven demand; red ocean = demand proven but profits competed away and late-stage sophistication. Both "pretty shitty options." The sweet spot sits near the red end: proven market + a carved segment nobody owns. A purple ocean is *created*, not found. Examples: (a) weight loss specifically for postmenopausal African-American women — a multi-billion market compressed to a segment with virtually zero direct competitors; (b) Primal Queen — rode the carnivore trend (picked up steam early 2022, peaked at 4.5M monthly searches Jan 2025) but carved out beef-organ supplements for women in specific life stages (postpartum, menopause); (c) a nootropics company targeting entrepreneurs specifically — "I bought it not because it was the best nootropic. I bought it because it was very specifically for me"; (d) pillowcases positioned against overnight acne rather than better sleep. The segment doesn't have to be demographic — it can be a single angle.

10. **Saturation litmus test: public chatter = too late.** "As soon as people start talking about something, you can probably assume that you're too late." Insiders run tactics months or years before they show up on Twitter/YouTube. Example: "native statics" got hyped as the new meta, yet they'd been running for literal decades on native ad platforms — and performance declined across many accounts as soon as the hype started.

11. **Principles are timeless; tactics decay. Disguise your marketing.** Gary Halbert story: sales letters failing → he observed that people open mail standing over the trash can, keeping only what looks relevant/important and binning anything promotional → so he made mail that didn't look like a promotion. That $2 direct-mail offer ran in the '60s/'70s, made over $100,000/day (over half a million in today's dollars), with 20 full-time employees whose sole job was cashing mailed-in checks. The same principle powers today's top assets: advertorials, quiz funnels, native ads. "Being able to create marketing content that doesn't look like marketing content is a superpower."

12. **Contrast and disguise are not contradictory — balance both.** Contrast gets you noticed; disguise keeps people from feeling sold to. "If you take nothing away from this video... these two things principally are going to make you a lot of money."

13. **Universal truth #1 — No clean hands.** Get your hands dirty. To scale you must first do things that don't scale: write the copy yourself, make the ads yourself, get in the ad account, post relentlessly. "I would not be the marketer that I am now if I just tried to outsource everything that required an ounce of effort." That's how you get your 10,000 hours.

14. **Universal truth #2 — Money loves speed.** Compress the feedback loop between idea → data → iteration. If an ad shows profitable early indicators with significant data, he starts iterating "halfway through the day." It's just the scientific method (question → hypothesis → test → data → iterate) with the cycle time squeezed to minimum. Departing employees consistently name one differentiator: "You guys move freakishly fast." Most companies move at the speed of snails; being small/young is a speed advantage — "lean into it."

15. **Universal truth #3 — Assume nothing.** "The only thing that is real is data. Anything before you get data is an assumption and should be left at the door." Don't assume your funnel or creatives are good; don't test things because *you* like them. "You don't know your customers as well as you probably think you do." The most profitable ads are frequently the ugly ones you'd never have picked.

16. **Universal truth #4 — Comparison is theft.** Comparison can motivate, but for beginners with no results it just corrodes. His 50-failed-products / $9,000-loss stretch was made needlessly miserable by comparing himself to others; then everything flipped in 2–3 months (a $20,000 bank balance that felt like the world). "Stay in your lane... the timing is always perfect. You just have to trust the process."

17. **Universal truth #5 — Break the rules (after learning them).** Most marketing "rules" were invented by a regular person decades ago and never re-examined. Learning the rules earns you the right to break them. Test everything, however silly. "If you think differently, then the world will change around you." Also good for the ego: when a test that contradicts your beliefs wins, you learn you don't know everything.

### Section 2 — How to create

18. **Copy is the foundation of all marketing success.** Two things get created: copy (the words) and creative (the visuals around the words). "If you write better copy than anybody else, you will destroy them long term."

19. **Copy is not content — they're opposite ends of a spectrum.** Direct response ←→ brand. Content's job is engagement (views, likes, clicks); copy's sole job is sales. The best attention-getters are usually not the best converters — he knows people with under 10,000 combined followers making more money than him because their copy converts. Peter Kell tested beautiful VSLs that were really content, not conversion copy: "Every single one of them bombed." "Most content creators are broke, because they have no idea how to write copy."

20. **Research is the foundation of copy.** Goal: understand customers better than they understand themselves. AI now compresses deep psychographic research into ~30 minutes (his "Foundational Docs" SOP — copy/paste into AI; DM "foundation" to @markbuildsbrands to get it).

21. **Write in Voice of Customer (VOC).** You're not writing for you; use the customer's language and phraseology. Copy is like a foreign language — counterintuitive if you learned to write in English class. "I would never hire a copywriter who has an English degree or a journalism degree."

22. **The old-school time split: 80% research, 5% writing, 15% chiefing.** Writing is the easy part. Chiefing = reviewing the copy through the lens of the customer and the lens of a copywriter.

23. **Write to ONE stage of awareness.** Unaware → problem-aware → solution-aware → product-aware → most-aware. Mixing stages means talking to multiple people at once, which is "the whole crux of the issue with copywriting" — write as if speaking to one hyper-specific person. You can *move* a reader from problem-aware to solution-aware to product-aware within a piece, but you can't address an unaware person the way you address a product-aware one.

24. **Also write to a specific level of consciousness, then engineer the emotional delta.** Borrowing David Hawkins' map of consciousness: (a) meet the prospect exactly where they calibrate (e.g., fear — the dominant level of the survival niche); (b) raise them to a higher state (courage, neutrality, willingness) so they *feel* what solved looks like; (c) bring them back down to their current reality. The gap between "where they could have been" and "where they are" — the emotional delta — is what drives action. Analogy: the entrepreneur who got rich, then went broke again, hurts far more the second time, because now he knows what rich feels like.

25. **The best research source is your own customers' words — talk to them.** Most e-com entrepreneurs never speak with a customer. Mark has a team calling customers and listens to transcripts constantly; he took one customer's exact phrase describing their problem, used it word-for-word as a hook, and it became one of the most profitable ads in his entire ad account. Forums (Reddit, Facebook groups, TikTok) are second-tier next to real customer conversations.

26. **Spend 90% of your time on the hook.** The first of anything is the most important: first 3–5 seconds of a video ad, the subject line of an email, the headline of a sales letter. "If you don't get people hooked in initially, you're never going to have them."

27. **PIG framework — Punch In the Gut (credited to copywriter Chris Adod).** The opening line must be so vivid, visceral, and emotional it lands like a punch. His most repeated feedback to copywriters: "We have to make this punch harder."

28. **Clarity over cleverness — always.** The biggest hook mistake is trying to be clever or impressive. From his best copy mentor: clarity wins every day of the week. Bring copy to a 5th-grade reading level (8th at most — never beyond). Plain, clear, direct copy outconverts "super duper indirect clever direct-response-on-steroids" copy.

29. **A video hook has three weighted ingredients: copy ~80%, scroll-stopper visual ~15%, audio ~5%.** Use all three. Scroll-stopper test: find clips that make someone say out loud "What the f— did I just watch?" (Show it to your mom; if that's her reaction, put it in Ads Manager.) Audio (voiceover/music/AI voice) is the smallest needle-mover.

30. **Swipe philosophy, not copy.** Swiping = rewriting winning ads for your niche. Never swipe blindly / one-to-one. Instead, break the winning ad down into the framework of *why* it worked — the underlying psychological principles — and bank those. Frameworks generalize to any ad in any niche; you can feed them to AI to generate millions of good variations. One-to-one swiping is the pump-and-dump dropshipping of copy: "it's going to work until it doesn't. Then what?" Principles-first thinking also makes you better at diagnosing your own ads.

31. **Remove unknown variables — sell proven things.** Every launch carries dozens of variables affecting profitability: media buying, creative, copy, product, market, the algorithm's mood ("I could probably jot down 30 or 40"). The most commonly violated rule: launching an unproven product, which adds the worst variable of all — not knowing if anyone wants it. "The most successful marketers that I know, they're feening for proven markets." "Success is not a game of addition, it's a game of subtraction. Remove all the [junk] and the success will find you."

32. **Ugly ads = pretty profits.** Aesthetics are incredibly overrated in paid advertising; what looks best is usually not what converts (another reason content creators go broke — cinematic masterpieces). His most successful ads ever "look like [crap]." The mechanism isn't ugliness itself: "stop optimizing for aesthetics, start optimizing for clarity." The ugly winners win because the copy is good and the message is unmistakably clear.

### Section 3 — The economics

33. **Business economics = three sectors: ads, funnel, back end.** (Back end = everything after the first purchase.)

34. **Ad economics (soft metrics: spend, CPM, CPC, CTR): get the cheapest traffic at the highest quality.** Cheap alone is a trap — run ads in India and you'll see $0.50 CPMs, sub-cent CPCs, and zero buyers. Qualification for your offer matters as much as cost. True for e-com, affiliate, and high-ticket alike.

35. **Funnel economics: maximize conversions AND average order value simultaneously.** Some argue high AOV conflicts with LTV plays; Mark says you can have both — high AOV front end plus solid repeat purchase rate.

36. **Dan Kennedy's law: "The guy that can spend the most to acquire a customer wins."** Worked example: you and a competitor both pay $50 CAC. His customer is worth $80; yours is worth $100. That "measly" $20 margin means you can bid up to $70 per customer while he's capped at $50 — you outbid him in the auction. Acquisition is the hard part; once you own the customer (email, phone, data) you can resell them forever. Maximizing AOV literally buys you scaling room.

37. **Back-end economics: one variable — maximize LTV.** (AOV = per-order; LTV = total collected per customer; a $100 repurchase makes LTV $200 while AOV stays $100.) Every business that scaled profitably or went public "mastered LTV... mastered retention." Low-ticket route = subscriptions (big supplement brands force subscriptions on their own sites because they know CAC only rises). High-ticket route = ascension: finish the $5K program → get upsold into the $10K program.

38. **LTV:CAC targets: minimum 3:1; high-ticket should shoot for 7:1–10:1.** At 3:1, a $300 LTV means never spending more than $100 to acquire. "One of the most basic baseline measurements of how healthy your business really is." (KPIs differ by model — high-ticket ≠ e-com ≠ affiliate.)

39. **Don't trust ad-platform data — post-iOS 14 it lies.** Misattribution runs 5–10% on the low side, 30–50% on the high side; a recent iOS update made landing-page views and content views "completely inaccurate." Without a source-of-truth attribution layer (he names Triple Whale for e-com, Hyros (the transcript garbles this as 'Highrise'), Wicked Reports, WeTrack) you will "optimize your ad account around shitty ads... and tell Facebook, hey, go spend on all those shitty ads." Know your numbers with 20/20 clarity; most marketers are flying blind. "I'm psychotic about my data."

40. **Entropy: everything breaks as you scale — inject energy.** Scaling doesn't make things easier; the challenges change (CAC rises, creative demand rises). Businesses, like the universe, drift from order toward chaos (second law of thermodynamics); winning ads, offers, and copy all eventually die — "everything that is created and has life eventually is going to die." The only counter is injecting energy, in exactly three forms: (1) your own hours (finite), (2) human capital (hires), (3) systems and AI. Bonus: business partners who poke holes in your operation are injecting energy — they find problems before they blow up in your face.

41. **Get surgical at identifying constraints — metrics are indicators, not constraints.** Picture the business as a garden hose with kinks; traffic in one end, sales out the other. In his BBA Monday reviews, a common pattern: ad metrics look fantastic (CPC, CPM, CTR all great) but funnel conversion rate is low. "That's not a constraint. That is an indicator of a constraint" — it tells you people aren't buying, not *why*. You need KPIs on every funnel step and deep understanding of step-to-step drop-off. Real example: a student's checkout-page drop-off was massive — because his payment processor wasn't set up.

42. **Before you launch anything, try to break it.** Don't just QC it — actively try to break it, "because if you don't, your customers will." The number of people launching funnels they never once tested themselves is "honestly insane."

43. **Marketing "intuition" doesn't exist — it's compressed data.** What experts call intuition is pattern recognition from thousands of tests and data points. Mark, at ~20,000 hours, can *feel* whether an ad will work — and is "still wrong all the time," as are people with 50,000 hours. A beginner's "I can just feel this product will win" is cognitive bias, not intuition — "that's you trying to get lucky." Get your 10,000 hours first, then talk about intuition.

### Section 4 — The multiplier (AI)

44. **AI multiplies everything above — it is not just a copy/creative tool.** Five years ago this section wouldn't exist. Most people view AI in a closed box ("AI = ChatGPT"); in reality AI can optimize or improve every single metric in the economics section. This isn't coming — "it is here and it's only going to get crazier."

45. **Treat AI education as an urgent, personal obligation.** "If you're not obsessing about AI now, you're late and you need to catch up." He obsesses daily and *still* feels behind after 5 minutes on AI Twitter. Nobody will push your AI education for you.

46. **The AI customer-avatar technique (his one concrete gift).** If you have no customers yet (or can't call them): build the Foundational Docs research, prompt an AI to fully adopt those beliefs as your customer, upload all research, then put in AirPods, go for a walk, and hold a voice conversation with your "customer." Create separate avatars per buying angle and interrogate them all. He's done this across multiple brands: "every single time I say, damn, I should be doing this more often."

47. **Andromeda proves the stakes.** Meta rebuilt its ad-ranking algorithm (Andromeda) last year, largely because the flood of AI-generated ad content overwhelmed the old sorting system — and the new system itself uses AI to sort ads. When a multi-billion-dollar company rebuilds its core algorithm over AI content volume, the shift is present-tense.

48. **The five levels of AI use — climb them.** Level 1: LLM chat (ChatGPT, Claude) — where most people are stuck. Level 2: workflows (early n8n-style, semi-agentic). Level 3: agents — the current era (he cites Anthropic shipping Claude Code and Cowork) — autonomous multi-step task execution. Level 4: agentic systems — teams of agents working toward one goal (almost nobody is here). Level 5: AGI. Push yourself up the ladder.

49. **There is a closing window of opportunity.** Right now, ordinary people can figure out how to make money with AI before mass adoption; the window shrinks daily while the barrier to entry drops daily. He expects AGI plausibly within 5 years. "Be more bullish on AI... this is the greatest time in history to ever be alive."

## How to apply this to any brand

The operator audit — run these five steps in order before any launch, and re-run them whenever performance stalls. Each step maps directly to the numbered lessons above.

**Step 1 — Contrast audit vs. competitors (#2–4, #8, #11–12).** Pull the top 5–10 competitors' ads, emails, and organic content (ad libraries, subscribed inboxes, feeds). List every convention they share: visual style, hook openers, promise framing, format, length, tone, offer structure. For each variable, ask the entry question — "How can I do the exact opposite?" — because the job is not marketing *better*, it's looking *nothing like anybody else*. Then apply the disguise check: does the creative read as a promotion? If yes, rework it toward the Halbert standard (marketing content that doesn't look like marketing content). Contrast gets you noticed; disguise keeps people from feeling sold to — ship creative that scores on both.

**Step 2 — Purple-ocean check (#6–7, #9–10, #23).** Confirm demand is proven: people already buy this category, so you channel desire rather than create it (never build a plan that requires educating the market). Then carve a hyper-specific segment nobody owns — a demographic, life stage, use case, or even a single angle. The pass test: would a prospect say "this is very specifically for me"? Run the saturation litmus alongside: if the niche or tactic is already being talked about publicly on social media, assume you're late — extract the underlying principle and find the next carve. Finally, confirm each creative speaks to exactly ONE awareness stage of that segment.

**Step 3 — Unit-economics floor check (#33–39).** Compute LTV (total collected per customer, not per order) and current or projected CAC. Floor: LTV:CAC ≥ 3:1; high-ticket offers should shoot for 7:1–10:1. If you're below the floor, the fix is not "better ads" — it's AOV (bundles, upsells) and back end (subscription/continuity for low-ticket, ascension ladder for high-ticket), because the operator who can spend the most to acquire a customer wins the auction, and entropy guarantees CAC rises with scale. Before trusting a dollar of platform-reported results, install a source-of-truth attribution layer — platform numbers can be off 5–50% post-iOS 14.

**Step 4 — Hook time allocation (#20–29).** For every creative unit, spend 90% of working time on the hook (first 3–5 seconds of video, subject line, headline). Within a video hook, weight the effort copy ~80% / scroll-stopper visual ~15% / audio ~5%. Source hook language from verbatim customer words — recorded calls and reviews beat forums, forums beat guessing — and write in Voice of Customer at a 5th–8th-grade reading level regardless of how sophisticated the audience is (a scrolling PhD is still a scrolling human). Edit with two blades: "make this punch harder" (PIG) and "clarity over cleverness." If there are no customers to mine yet, run the AI customer-avatar technique (#46) per buying angle.

**Step 5 — Break the funnel before launch (#31, #41–42).** Adversarial pre-launch pass on the entire path, on mobile: click the ad, time the page load, submit the form / add to cart, pay with a real card or book the real calendar, confirm the pixel fires on the true conversion event, and trigger every email/SMS automation. Simultaneously remove unknown variables: proven product or offer, boring funnel, all the novelty budget spent on creative contrast — success is subtraction. Post-launch, diagnose with the indicator-vs-constraint discipline: a bad metric tells you *where* people leak, never *why* — instrument every funnel step so you can find the actual kink in the hose.

Worked micro-examples:

- **DTC supplement brand (e-commerce).** Contrast audit finds every competitor running polished studio UGC with "3 reasons why" hooks → ship ugly, native-looking POV creative opened with a customer's verbatim review phrase. Purple-ocean: "sleep gummies" is red ocean; "sleep support for night-shift nurses" is a carve. Economics: $80 AOV with weak repeat purchase → LTV ≈ $95 → CAC ceiling ~$32 at 3:1 — too thin to scale, so a subscription flow is built to lift LTV *before* budget increases. Break-the-funnel: place a live mobile test order and confirm the post-purchase upsell and the purchase pixel both fire.

- **High-ticket B2B service ($15K implementation retainer for manufacturers).** Contrast audit shows every competitor running polished-guru webinar funnels → the opposite is an unpolished founder-on-the-shop-floor video and an application page. Purple-ocean: "marketing agency" owns nothing; "demand gen specifically for mid-size manufacturers" can own 100% of its carve. Economics: $15K front end with no continuation offer is fragile at the prescribed 7:1–10:1 high-ticket bar (CAC ceiling $1.5K–$2.1K) — design the ascension/retainer step before scaling spend, and track closed deals in the CRM as ground truth, not platform-reported leads. Hooks: mine sales-call recordings for the exact sentence prospects use to describe their pipeline problem.

- **B2B SaaS ($50/mo project-management tool).** Contrast audit: every competitor demos dashboards over royalty-free music → the opposite hook dramatizes the painful moment ("the 47-message thread about one deadline") with zero product footage in the first 5 seconds. Economics: 14-month average retention → LTV $700 → CAC ceiling ~$233 at 3:1; retention (the back end) is the scaling lever, not cheaper clicks. Break-the-funnel: sign up with a fresh email on mobile, verify the trial onboarding sequence actually sends, and confirm the trial-start event — not the pageview — is what the ad platform optimizes toward.
